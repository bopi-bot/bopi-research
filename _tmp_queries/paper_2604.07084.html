Flow Motion Policy:
Manipulator Motion Planning with Flow Matching Models

Davood Soleymanzadeh1, Xiao Liang2,∗, and Minghui Zheng1,∗ 1Davood Soleymanzadeh and Minghui Zheng are with the J. Mike Walker ’66 Department of Mechanical Engineering, Texas A&M University, College Station, TX 77843, USA (e-mail: davoodso@tamu.edu; mhzheng@tamu.edu).2Xiao Liang is with the Zachry Department of Civil and Environmental Engineering, Texas A&M University, College Station, TX 77843 USA (e-mail: xliang@tamu.edu).∗ Corresponding Authors.This work was supported by the USA National Science Foundation under Grant No. 2527316 and No. 2422826. Abstract

Open-loop end-to-end neural motion planners have recently been proposed to improve motion planning for robotic manipulators. These methods enable planning directly from sensor observations without relying on a privileged collision checker during planning. However, many existing methods generate only a single path for a given workspace across different runs, and do not leverage their open-loop structure for inference-time optimization. To address this limitation, we introduce Flow Motion Policy, an open-loop, end-to-end neural motion planner for robotic manipulators that leverages the stochastic generative formulation of flow matching methods to capture the inherent multi-modality of planning datasets. By modeling a distribution over feasible paths, Flow Motion Policy enables efficient inference-time best-of-NN sampling. The method generates multiple end-to-end candidate paths, evaluates their collision status after planning, and executes the first collision-free solution. We benchmark the Flow Motion Policy against representative sampling-based and neural motion planning methods. Evaluation results demonstrate that Flow Motion Policy improves planning success and efficiency, highlighting the effectiveness of stochastic generative policies for end-to-end motion planning and inference-time optimization. I Introduction

Motion planning plays an important role in robotic manipulation systems, operating alongside high-level control policies to ensure safe and feasible actions. The objective is to compute a valid, collision-free path that connects an initial configuration to a desired goal configuration [25, 34]. Achieving fast and reliable motion planning for manipulators in unstructured environments remains a challenging problem. Decades of research have established many algorithms to achieve this, among which probabilistically complete sampling-based [21] and trajectory optimization [45] algorithms have been widely used for robotic manipulators.

Sampling-based planners (SBPs) construct a tree in the configuration space to connect the start and goal configurations by iteratively generating random samples within the configuration space, steering towards the newly sampled configurations, and collision checking the resulting connections [20, 17]. Due to their stochastic sampling process, SBPs may generate different plans for the same planning problem across multiple runs. However, these planners rely on a privileged collision checker during planning, and collision checking is the main bottleneck in motion planning, accounting for up to 90% of the computation time [8], which makes parallelism and inference-time optimization challenging [14, 39]. Although recent advanced variants [43, 12] introduce heuristic-based informed samplers to bias the sampling process towards the goal to reduce the number of collision queries, such heuristics are challenging to design for high-dimensional configuration spaces.

Trajectory optimization methods [45, 16] iteratively refine an initial trajectory, often a straight-line interpolation between the start and goal configurations, using numerical optimization techniques. These methods enforce constraints, including obstacle avoidance and trajectory smoothness, during optimization. Although trajectory optimization approaches are amenable to parallelization and can benefit from best-of-NN initialization strategies [38], they still require a privileged collision checker to provide both collision status and gradient information throughout the optimization process.

Recently, deep learning methods have been leveraged to address the limitations of sampling-based and trajectory optimization planners. In sampling-based planning, neural networks have been proposed as neural informed samplers [29, 32, 15, 33] to bias tree expansion towards the goal, reducing the number of collision checks. Neural networks have also been used as implicit collision checkers [18, 35], enabling faster collision queries. However, these approaches still require collision querying during planning, which limits their efficiency in best-of-NN sampling. For trajectory optimization, learning-based methods have been leveraged to generate high-quality initial trajectories [3, 41], which can help to avoid local minima and accelerate convergence. Nevertheless, these methods also depend on collision checkers to provide collision status and gradient information during optimization.

Moreover, another line of work leverages deep learning for open-loop, end-to-end motion planning [31, 7, 42], eliminating the need for a privileged collision checker during planning. These methods learn a direct mapping from the planning problem information (e.g., start configuration, goal configuration, and environment observations) to a feasible path. However, most existing approaches are deterministic policies that produce a single path for a given planning problem. As a result, they cannot exploit best-of-NN sampling at inference time.

We present Flow Motion Policy, an end-to-end neural motion planner that leverages the stochastic generative formulation of flow matching frameworks [22] to enable best-of-N sampling during inference, as demonstrated in Figure LABEL:fig:openning. Flow Motion Policy generates multiple candidate trajectories and applies a light-weight inference-time optimization to select the first collision-free solution for execution. This design allows Flow Motion Policy to operate both as a direct end-to-end planning policy (without planning), and as a planner enhanced by best-of-NN sampling at inference time.

Our main contributions are:

  • •

    We leverage the stochastic generative formulation of flow matching [22] to enable light-weight inference-time optimization for end-to-end motion planning. This capability allows best-of-NN sampling during inference to output the first collision-free plan among the planned paths.

  • •

    We show that the proposed end-to-end planner can be used both as a direct end-to-end planning policy and as a planner enhanced with inference-time best-of-NN sampling for robotic manipulators’ motion planning.

  • •

    We evaluate Flow Motion Policy on held-out evaluation tasks, and demonstrate its effectiveness compared to state-of-the-art motion planners.

The rest of the paper is organized as follows. Section II presents an overview of prior work in motion planning for robotic manipulators. Section III describes the proposed Flow Motion Policy framework. Section IV reports experimental results and compares Flow Motion Policy with benchmark motion planning methods. Finally, Section V concludes the paper.

II Related Works

We survey related work on motion planning for robotic manipulators, and outline the main differences of the proposed framework and the state of the art.

Motion Planning with Collision Checking. Over the years, numerous motion planning algorithms have been developed for robotic manipulators [34], with sampling-based [25] and trajectory optimization [45] planners being the most widely used.

Sampling-based planners iteratively build a search tree over the robot’s configuration space, stochastically generate samples and expanding the tree towards the planning goal [21]. Due to their stochastic sampling process, these planners are inherently multi-modal, meaning that different runs can produce different feasible trajectories for the same planning problem. This property can, in principle, be exploited for parallel planning and best-of-NN sampling. However, planners require a collision checker during planning, which is the dominant computational bottleneck in planning [14, 39]. Hand-crafted informed [43, 36, 12] and neural informed samplers [29, 15, 28, 32, 33] have been proposed to guide the planning towards goal, reducing the number of collision queries. However, these informed samplers still inherit the sequential nature of sampling-based planning algorithms and require a collision checker in the tree construction process, which is computationally expensive to perform best-of-NN sampling during inference time [32]. In contrast, Flow Motion Policy performs collision checking in parallel to enable efficient best-of-NN sampling during inference.

Trajectory optimization planners start from an initial trajectory, which may be in collision, and enforce planning constraints and utilize numerical optimization to push the initial path to collision-free spaces while satisfying smoothness constraints [45, 16, 23, 6]. However, these planners depend strongly on the quality of the initial trajectory and can often converge to local minima. To mitigate this issue, deep learning methods have recently been leveraged to learn the underlying distribution of planning datasets to warm-start trajectory optimization [3, 41, 24]. Although trajectory optimization planners can operate on a batch of initial solutions and select one that satisfies planning constraints, they still require collision checking during planning, which increases computation time. In contrast, Flow Motion Policy performs collision checking in parallel during inference for efficient best-of-NN sampling.

End-to-end Neural Motion Planning. Deep learning approaches have also been explored for end-to-end motion planning, where neural networks plan motions in an open-loop manner without relying on privileged collision checkers [31, 42, 10, 11]. However, most existing approaches are deterministic motion policies and therefore do not support inference-time optimization through best-of-NN sampling. Neural MP [7] addresses this limitation by adopting a Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM) as the policy output representation to facilitate best-of-NN sampling during inference. Nevertheless, GMMs have limited capacity to represent the multi-modality inherent in motion planning problems. In contrast, Flow Motion Policy employs flow matching [22] to model this multi-modality and to support best-of-NN sampling during inference.

Diffusion and flow matching models in robotics and motion planning. Diffusion and flow models have emerged as powerful deep generative models in robotics [5]. Diffusion and flow-based behavior cloning can learn complex policies and and have demonstrated strong performance in real-world robotic applications, spanning from task-specific settings [9] to generalist policies [1, 2]. Diffusion models have also been explored for motion planning, including end-to-end approaches [41] and for warm-starting trajectory optimization algorithms [3]. In this work, we employ a flow-based policy as the decoder head of the proposed neural motion planner, enabling best-of-NN sampling at inference time [44].

III Flow Motion Policy

In this section, we introduce Flow Motion Policy, an end-to-end motion planner that utilizes a flow matching head to provide best-of-NN sampling at inference time. Figure 1 illustrates the structure of the proposed framework.

III-A Problem Definition

We consider the motion planning problem for a robotic manipulator with configuration 𝐪∈ℝn\mathbf{q}\in\mathbb{R}^{n}, where nn denotes the manipulator’s DOF. Given a start configuration 𝐪start\mathbf{q}_{\text{start}}, goal configuration 𝐪goal\mathbf{q}_{\text{goal}}, and an environment observation representation 𝒫\mathcal{P}, the objective is to generate a collision-free path (q={𝐪start,𝐪1,⋯,𝐪goal}q=\{\mathbf{q}_{\text{start}},\mathbf{q}_{1},\cdots,\mathbf{q}_{\text{goal}}\}) connecting 𝐪start\mathbf{q}_{\text{start}} and 𝐪goal\mathbf{q}_{\text{goal}}. Instead of solving for a single path, we learn a conditional distribution that predicts the distribution over joint configuration increments over the planning horizon (HH):

pθ​(δ​𝐪t:t+H−1|𝐪t,𝐪goal,𝒫),\displaystyle p_{\theta}(\delta\mathbf{q}_{t:t+H-1}|\mathbf{q}_{t},\mathbf{q}_{\text{goal}},\mathcal{P}), (1)

where 𝐪t\mathbf{q}_{t} denotes current timestep configuration, and θ\theta denotes the parameters of the neural motion planner, which is detailed in Section III-D. The path is generated autoregressively by predicting configuration increments:

δ​𝐪t:t+H−1=πθ​(𝐪t,𝐪goal,𝒫).\displaystyle\delta\mathbf{q}_{t:t+H-1}=\pi_{\theta}(\mathbf{q}_{t},\mathbf{q}_{\text{goal}},\mathcal{P}). (2)

In the following sections, we learn this path distribution using flow-matching frameworks.

Flow Motion Policy is evaluated autoregressively in an open-loop manner. The planning policy is rolled out for a fixed number of steps, where each predicted joint action chunk is auto-regressively fed back as input for subsequent action joint predictions. III-C Best-of-NN Sampling

We augment Flow Motion Policy with a lightweight inference-time optimization to improve the planning efficiency of the base policy [7]. Specifically, leveraging the stochastic generative formulation of flow matching [22], we sample NN candidate paths connecting the initial and goal configurations to generate a set of candidate trajectories 𝒬∗={qi}i=1N\mathcal{Q}^{*}=\{q_{i}\}_{i=1}^{N}, where each trajectory qi={𝐪t(i)}t=1Tq_{i}=\{\mathbf{q}_{t}^{(i)}\}_{t=1}^{T} represents a sequence of robot configurations over the pre-defined roll-out length TT. We evaluate each trajectory using a collision checker and select the first collision-free path:

q∗=arg⁡minqi⁡𝒞​(qi),\displaystyle q^{*}=\arg\min_{q_{i}}\mathcal{C}(q_{i}), (7)

where the cost function 𝒞​(qi)\mathcal{C}(q_{i}) is defined as:

𝒞​(qi)=∑t=1T𝕀​{d​(𝐪ti,𝒫)<δsafe},\displaystyle\mathcal{C}(q_{i})=\sum_{t=1}^{T}\mathbb{I}\{d(\mathbf{q}_{t}^{i},\mathcal{P})<\delta_{\text{safe}}\}, (8)

where d​(𝐪ti,𝒫)d(\mathbf{q}_{t}^{i},\mathcal{P}) denotes the minimum signed distance between the robot at configuration 𝐪ti\mathbf{q}_{t}^{i} and the environment representation 𝒫\mathcal{P}, 𝕀(.)\mathbb{I}(.) is an indicator function, and δsafe\delta_{\text{safe}} is the safety distance threshold. In real-world deployment, d​(𝐪ti,𝒫)d(\mathbf{q}_{t}^{i},\mathcal{P}) is computed using robot signed distance function over environment representation. In the simulation, the efficient collision utilities of cuRobo [38] are leveraged for batched collision checking of all candidate paths. Algorithm 3 details the best-of-NN sampling procedure.

Embedding robot and workspace planning information. The current and goal configurations are embedded via a shared multi-layer perceptron (MLP):

𝐡t=MLP​(𝐪t),\displaystyle\mathbf{h}_{t}=\text{MLP}(\mathbf{q}_{t}), (9)
𝐡g​o​a​l=MLP​(𝐪g​o​a​l),\displaystyle\mathbf{h}_{goal}=\text{MLP}(\mathbf{q}_{goal}), (10)

where 𝐪t∈ℝ6\mathbf{q}_{t}\in\mathbb{R}^{6}, 𝐪g​o​a​l∈ℝ6\mathbf{q}_{goal}\in\mathbb{R}^{6}, 𝐡t∈ℝd\mathbf{h}_{t}\in\mathbb{R}^{d}, and 𝐡g​o​a​l∈ℝd\mathbf{h}_{goal}\in\mathbb{R}^{d} denote the configuration at time step tt, the goal configuration, and their corresponding embeddings, respectively, and dd is the embedding size. Also, robot and scene point clouds are down-sampled and embedded with set abstraction layers from PointNet++ [27]:

𝐡r=PointNet++​(𝒫r),\displaystyle\mathbf{h}_{r}=\text{PointNet++}(\mathcal{P}_{r}), (11)
𝐡w=PointNet++​(𝒫w),\displaystyle\mathbf{h}_{w}=\text{PointNet++}(\mathcal{P}_{w}), (12)

where 𝒫r∈ℝNr×3\mathcal{P}_{r}\in\mathbb{R}^{N_{r}\times 3} is the point cloud of robotic manipulator, 𝒫w∈ℝNw×3\mathcal{P}_{w}\in\mathbb{R}^{N_{w}\times 3} is the point cloud of planning scene, 𝐡r∈ℝKr×d\mathbf{h}_{r}\in\mathbb{R}^{K_{r}\times d}, and 𝐡w∈ℝKw×d\mathbf{h}_{w}\in\mathbb{R}^{K_{w}\times d} are their corresponding embeddings, respectively.

Transformer encoder. Each of the planning embeddings, including (𝐡t,𝐡goal,𝐡r,𝐡w\mathbf{h}_{t},~\mathbf{h}_{\text{goal}},~\mathbf{h}_{r},~\mathbf{h}_{w}), corresponds to elements of planning observation 𝐨={𝐪t,𝐪goal,𝒫𝐫,𝒫𝐰}\mathbf{o}=\{\mathbf{q}_{t},\mathbf{q_{\text{goal}},\mathcal{P}_{r},\mathcal{P}_{w}}\}. These embeddings are augmented with a learnable token embedding: et,egoal,er,ew∈ℝde_{t},~e_{\text{goal}},~e_{r},~e_{w}\in\mathbb{R}^{d}, which is added to each respective feature to encode its semantic role. The resulting representations are then treated as tokens and fed into the transformer encoder input.

Flow head. The flow head is instantiated as a multi-layer perceptron (MLP) that operates on learnable action tokens 𝐱^0∈ℝH\hat{\mathbf{x}}^{0}\in\mathbb{R}^{H}. The output of transformer encoder is also fed into the flow head to enable the model to incorporate planning information. The MLP predicts the continuous-time vector field 𝐯t\mathbf{v}_{t}, which is subsequently used to generate actions through flow-based sampling.

IV Evaluation

In this section, we present the deployment of Flow Motion Policy and evaluate it against representative sampling-based and neural motion planning methods. All models are implemented using PyTorch [26]. Evaluations and experiments are conducted on an NVIDIA RTX 4080 GPU.

IV-A Flow Motion Policy Training

We leverage an LLM-based workspace generator [31] to create a large number of motion planning environments, and leverage cuRobo [38], a fast, optimization-based motion planner, for planning data collection to train Flow Motion Policy. Algorithm 1 details the training process of the policy.

IV-B Benchmarks and Planning Metrics

Benchmarks. We compare the performance of Flow Motion Policy with a set of representative sampling-based and neural motion planning approaches. The benchmarks considered in this study are summarized as follows:

  • •

    Bi-Directional RRT (Bi-RRT) [19]: Bi-RRT grows two trees simultaneously from the start and goal in the configuration space. The trees attempt to connect to each other during the planning process to find a feasible path between the start and goal configurations is obtained. We use the Open Motion Planning Library (OMPL) [37] implementation of Bi-RRT for evaluation.

  • •

    Batch Informed Trees (BIT*) [12]: BIT* incrementally builds an implicit random geometric graph using batches of samples and performs a heuristic-guided search to explore the configuration space. By utilizing an admissible heuristic, BIT* focuses sampling within an informed subset of the configuration space to improve the current solution and reduce the number of samples and collision queries during planning. We utilize the Open Motion Planning Library (OMPL) [37] implementation of BiT* for evaluation.

  • •

    Motion Planning Networks (MPNets) [29]: MPNets is a neural informed sampler that produces goal-oriented samples within sampling-based planning frameworks. We implemented MPNets based on the official implementation and modified it to be compatible with the UR5e robotic manipulator used in our experiments.

  • •

    Spatial-informed Motion Planning Network (SIMPNet) [32]: SIMPNet is a kinematics-aware neural informed sampler that generates goal-directed samples for motion planning. The framework uses a cross-attention mechanism to condition the sampling network on workspace embeddings, enabling collision-aware and goal-directed sample generation.

  • •

    GAIDE [33]: GAIDE is a kinematics- and spatial-aware neural informed sampler that generates goal-directed samples for motion planning. The framework uses a binary attention mask to encode a graph representation of the robot embodiment and planning space into a transformer-based neural sampling network

  • •

    PerFACT [31]: PerFACT is an end-to-end neural motion planner that generates trajectories in an open-loop manner. The framework combines a point cloud encoder with a fusion action-chunking transformer architecture to integrate multiple sensing modalities for efficient motion planning.

Metrics. We access the performance of Flow Motion Policy using two standard evaluation metrics. These evaluation metrics are: planning time, and success rate. Planning time “T” measures the average time required to generate a path for each evaluation task, while Success rate “S” demonstrates the proportion of solved planning problems.
