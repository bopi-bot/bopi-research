## Problem

Learned world models combined with model predictive control (MPC) have emerged as a promising paradigm for zero-shot embodied control — enabling deployment in novel environments without task-specific reward functions or policy fine-tuning. Approaches like DreamerV2/V3, TD-MPC, IRIS, VJEPA2-AC, PLDM, and DINO-WM train latent world models on offline data via next-latent prediction, then select actions at test time by optimizing trajectories through the learned model.

However, two fundamental challenges remain:

1. **Prediction error accumulation**: Small one-step prediction errors compound during autoregressive rollouts, causing the world model's predictions to diverge from reality over long horizons. Even a high-accuracy single-step model can produce unusable predictions after many rollouts.

2. **Exponentially growing search space**: Even under a perfect model, planning becomes computationally intractable as the search tree grows exponentially with horizon length (the "curse of horizon").

Existing approaches to temporal abstraction are limited in complementary ways:
- **RL-based hierarchical methods** (options, skills, DreamerV3's hierarchical policies, HIQL, HILP) rely on task-specific rewards and struggle to generalize to environments and tasks beyond the training distribution.
- **Existing hierarchical MPC** methods are restricted to low-dimensional state spaces, hand-engineered representations, or known dynamics — they cannot operate on high-dimensional observations like raw pixels.
- **Single-level world-model planners** (VJEPA2-AC, DINO-WM, PLDM) succeed on short-horizon "greedy" tasks (e.g., grasping, reaching) but fail on non-greedy tasks where success requires temporarily moving away from the goal.

## Architecture

HWM (Hierarchical Planning with Latent World Models) introduces temporal hierarchy **at inference time** over pretrained task-agnostic world models. The key insight: rather than learning hierarchical policies, HWM plans over multiple timescales within a **shared latent space**, enabling direct coupling between levels.

### Two-Level World Models

HWM assumes access to two learned latent world models operating at different temporal resolutions within the **same latent space**:

- **Low-level model** $P^{(1)}\_{\theta}(z\_{t+1} \mid z\_{t}, a\_{t})$: predicts short-horizon transitions conditioned on primitive actions. Uses a pretrained, frozen encoder $E$ (ViT for VJEPA2-AC and DINO-WM, CNN for PLDM) to map observations to latent states $z\_{t} = E(s\_{t})$.

- **High-level model** $P^{(2)}\_{\phi}(z\_{t\_{k+1}} \mid z\_{t\_{k}}, l\_{t\_{k}})$: predicts long-horizon transitions conditioned on **latent macro-actions** $l\_{t}$ rather than primitive actions. Shares the same encoder $E$ and operates in the same latent space, ensuring subgoals are directly transferable between levels.

### Learned Macro-Actions via Action Encoder

Macro-actions are produced by a **learned action encoder** $A\_{\psi}$ (transformer-based for VJEPA2-AC and DINO-WM; MLP for PLDM) that compresses sequences of low-level actions between waypoint states into compact latent vectors. The CLS token is projected to the latent action space.

Unlike fixed-stride temporal abstraction, HWM does **not** assume a fixed high-level horizon — each high-level transition can correspond to a variable-length segment of low-level execution. This is critical for manipulation tasks where different phases require different time durations.

### Top-Down Hierarchical Planning

**High-level planning**: Given initial observation $s\_{1}$ and goal observation $s\_{g}$, both encoded into latent space, the high-level planner optimizes a sequence of $H$ latent macro-actions by minimizing:

$$\mathcal{E}\_{2}(\hat{l}\_{1:H}; z\_{1}, z\_{g}) \triangleq \left\| z\_{g} - P^{(2)}(\hat{l}\_{1:H}; z\_{1}) \right\|\_{1}$$

This is optimized via CEM (Cross-Entropy Method). Unrolling the optimized plan produces intermediate latent subgoals $\tilde{z}\_{i} = P^{(2)}(l^{*}\_{1:i}; z\_{1})$.

**Low-level planning**: Starting from the current latent state, the low-level planner optimizes a short horizon $h$ of primitive actions to reach the first subgoal:

$$\mathcal{E}\_{1}(\hat{a}\_{1:h}; z\_{1}, \tilde{z}\_{1}) \triangleq \left\| \tilde{z}\_{1} - P^{(1)}(\hat{a}\_{1:h}; z\_{1}) \right\|\_{1}$$

The agent re-plans every $k$ steps using the hierarchical planner (receding-horizon MPC).

### Encoder/Decoder Architecture (by domain)

| World Model | Encoder | WM Architecture | WM Params | Latent Action Dim |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| VJEPA2-AC (Franka) | ViT-g/16 (frozen) | ViT, ~300M params | ~300M | 4 |
| DINO-WM (Push-T) | DINOv2 (frozen) | ViT, 25M (low) / 75M (high) | 25M/75M | — |

A ViT decoder exists for visualization only — latent reconstructions into RGB images are used **solely** for qualitative analysis, never for planning or training.

## Training

### Overview

Training is **two-stage** and **domain-dependent**. The low-level world model is pretrained following the original method's recipe (VJEPA2-AC, DINO-WM, or PLDM). The high-level world model is then trained on top of the frozen encoder.

### Loss Functions

**Low-level training objective** — weighted sum of teacher-forcing and rollout losses:

$$L(\theta) := \gamma\_{\text{tf}} \cdot \mathcal{L}\_{\text{tf}}(\theta) + \gamma\_{\text{roll}} \cdot \mathcal{L}\_{\text{roll}}(\theta)$$

where $\mathcal{L}\_{\text{tf}}(\theta) = \frac{1}{T}\sum\_{k=1}^{T}\left\|\hat{z}\_{k+1} - z\_{k+1}\right\|\_{1}$ is the single-step next-latent prediction loss, and $\mathcal{L}\_{\text{roll}}(\theta) = \sum\_{j=2}^{T}\|P^{(1)}\_{\theta}(a\_{1:j}, z\_{1}) - z\_{j+1}\|\_{1}$ is a multi-step autoregressive rollout loss for mitigating compounding errors.

**High-level training objective** — pure teacher-forcing loss over waypoints:

$$\mathcal{L}\_{\text{tf}}(\phi, \psi) = \frac{1}{N}\sum\_{k=1}^{N}\left\|\hat{z}\_{t\_{k+1}} - z\_{t\_{k+1}}\right\|\_{1}$$

where the high-level model takes waypoint latent states and latent macro-actions as input.

### Per-Domain Training Details

**Franka Arm (VJEPA2-AC backbone)**:
- Data: ~96 hours from DROID + ~30 hours from RoboSet (total ~126 hours, ~130 hours stated in abstract)
- Observations: 256×256 RGB images + 7D end-effector proprioception (3 pos + 3 Euler angles + gripper)
- Actions: end-effector delta poses, mapped to joint deltas via IK
- Low-level: T=15 timesteps, batch 256, 200 epochs, $\gamma\_{\text{tf}}=1.0$, $\gamma\_{\text{roll}}=1.0$
- High-level: segments spanning 0.33–4 seconds, N=3 waypoints, batch 768, 120 epochs, $\gamma\_{\text{tf}}=1.0$, $\gamma\_{\text{roll}}=0.0$

**Push-T (DINO-WM backbone)**:
- Data: 18,500 offline trajectories from DINO-WM release
- Low-level: ViT with causal masking, context T=5 (each transition = 5-frame stride), 10D action (5×2), 100 epochs, batch 256
- High-level: segments of 25–70 timesteps, N=5 waypoints, scaled-up ViT (75M params: 10 layers, dim 768, MLP 3072, 12 heads), 500 epochs, batch 128

**Diverse Maze (PLDM backbone)**:
- Environment: MuJoCo PointMaze on 10×10 grid, 4D state (x, y, vx, vy), top-down 98×98 RGB input
- Data: 2000 episodes per map × 25 maps = 5M transitions, 100 steps per episode, action repeat 4
- Low-level: conv encoder (33k params) + conv predictor (20k params), trained jointly with VICReg regularization, T=15
- High-level: 60 timesteps per segment, 6 waypoints with fixed stride of 10, 8D latent actions via MLP encoder

### Hardware

Planning rollouts are executed in parallel across GPUs. No specific GPU model mentioned.

## Evaluation

### Franka Arm — Real-World Zero-Shot Manipulation (Table 1)

| Method | P&P Cup (w/ subgoals) | P&P Box (w/ subgoals) | P&P Cup | P&P Box | Drawer |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Octo | 20% | 10% | 0% | 0% | 43% |
| $\pi\_{0}$-FAST-DROID | — | — | 52% | 18% | — |
| $\pi\_{0.5}$-DROID | — | — | 68% | 36% | — |
| VJEPA2-AC (flat) | 80% | 80% | **0%** | **0%** | 30% |
| **VJEPA2-AC (HWM)** | 80% | 80% | **70%** | **60%** | **70%** |

Key: Without hierarchy, the flat planner achieves 0% on pick-and-place (non-greedy). With hierarchy, 70% on cup, 60% on box. Drawer: 30% → 70%. HWM outperforms VLA baselines ($\pi\_{0.5}$-DROID at 68%/36%) despite VLMs being pretrained on ~77× more robotic interaction data.

### Where Hierarchy Helps Most

The benefit of hierarchical planning scales with **task horizon** and **non-greediness**:
- Short-horizon greedy tasks (d=25 Push-T, easy maze): modest gains (+5–10pp)
- Long-horizon non-greedy tasks (pick-and-place, hard maze): massive gains (+39–70pp)
- Hierarchical planning also provides **compute efficiency**: 3–4× less inference-time compute for comparable or better success

### Ablation: Latent Action Encoding (Table 4)

Latent actions outperform delta-pose actions for high-level planning:
- Cosine similarity to expert behavior: 0.88 (latent) vs 0.80 (delta pose)
- L1 distance to expert: 0.080 (latent) vs 0.088 (delta pose)
- Learned latent actions capture non-greedy structure (e.g., move up then down) that delta-pose collapses

### Ablation: Latent Action Dimensionality (Fig. 7)

- dim < 4: high-level planner fails to produce valid plans (insufficient capacity)
- dim ≥ 4: valid plans, but too-high dimensions produce subgoals unreachable by low-level planner
- **Optimal: dim=4** for Franka tasks — expressive enough for useful trajectories, constrained enough to bias toward greedy-reachable subgoals

### Ablation: Long-Horizon Prediction Accuracy (Fig. 6)

- ≤1s horizon: low-level model more accurate
- ≥1.5s horizon: high-level single-step prediction outperforms low-level autoregressive rollouts
- Validates the hierarchical strategy: high-level for long-term guidance, low-level for short-term precision

## Reproduction Guide

1. **Clone the repo**: `git clone https://github.com/kevinghst/HWM_PLDM`
2. **Train a low-level world model** using the base method's recipe:
   - For VJEPA2-AC: use pretrained ViT-g/16 encoder, train ~300M ViT predictor for 200 epochs on DROID+RoboSet (batch 256, T=15, $\gamma\_{\text{tf}}=1.0$, $\gamma\_{\text{roll}}=1.0$)
   - For DINO-WM: use pretrained DINOv2 encoder, train 25M ViT for 100 epochs on Push-T dataset (batch 256, T=5)
   - For PLDM: train conv encoder + predictor jointly for ~100 epochs on maze data (5M transitions from 25 maps)
3. **Train the action encoder** (transformer-based for VJEPA2-AC/DINO-WM; MLP for PLDM) on action subsequences between waypoints
4. **Train the high-level world model** on top of the frozen encoder:
   - Sample variable-length trajectory segments
   - Select N waypoints (N=3 for Franka, N=5 for Push-T, 6 for Maze)
   - Train with teacher-forcing loss only ($\gamma\_{\text{roll}}=0.0$)
   - Franka: 120 epochs, batch 768, segments 0.33–4s
   - Push-T: 500 epochs, batch 128, segments 25–70 timesteps, scaled-up 75M ViT
   - Maze: reuse frozen low-level backbone, train higher-capacity conv net
5. **Configure CEM planning**:
   - High-level: 3000 CEM samples, prediction horizon 2 (Franka), latent action dim 4
   - Low-level: 800 CEM samples, prediction horizon 2 (Franka)
   - Replan every k=1 step (receding-horizon MPC)
6. **Evaluate**: provide current observation + goal observation (RGB + proprioception), run hierarchical MPC loop

## Notes

### Key Takeaways

- **Hierarchy at inference time, not training time**: HWM is a planning-only abstraction that operates on pretrained world models — no hierarchical policy learning, no skill discovery, no task-specific rewards. This makes it plug-and-play across diverse WM architectures.
- **Shared latent space is the coupling mechanism**: The key design choice enabling top-down hierarchy is that both levels operate in the same latent space. High-level predictions act as subgoals via direct latent-state matching ($\ell\_1$ distance), removing the need for inverse models or goal-conditioned value functions.
- **Latent action encoding matters**: A learned action encoder that compresses variable-length action sequences is significantly better than simple delta-pose representations, because it preserves non-greedy trajectory structure that displacement collapses.
- **Bottleneck regularization via dimensionality**: The latent action dimension acts as an implicit regularizer — too high and subgoals become unreachable by the low-level planner, too low and the high-level planner can't produce valid plans. dim=4 is the sweet spot for manipulation.
- **Model-agnostic**: HWM improved performance across VJEPA2-AC, DINO-WM, and PLDM — three fundamentally different world model architectures (ViT with pretrained backbone, ViT with frozen DINOv2, conv net trained jointly).

### Connection to Existing Notes

**unified-self-attention-memory-video-world-models.md** (Matrix-Game 3.0's unified self-attention memory): HWM uses a shared latent space across hierarchical levels, which is conceptually related to unified memory architectures. Both approaches aim to maintain a consistent representation that bridges different temporal scales. However, HWM's hierarchy is purely for planning — the encoder is shared and frozen, whereas unified memory architectures typically learn a single representation end-to-end. A natural extension would be whether unified self-attention memory could replace the separate low/high-level models with a single model that naturally operates at multiple scales.

**forward-inverse-asymmetry-easier-verification-than-prediction.md**: HWM's hierarchical structure implicitly exploits the forward-inverse asymmetry. The low-level planner performs forward prediction to evaluate action sequences, while the high-level planner generates subgoals that serve as verification targets. The key insight from HWM aligns with the asymmetry thesis: you don't need perfect long-horizon forward prediction — you just need the high level to produce *roughly correct* subgoals, and the low level can verify via short-horizon forward prediction. This mirrors how verification (short-horizon reachability) is easier than prediction (long-horizon accuracy).

**latent-intent-bottleneck-vla.md**: HWM's latent macro-actions serve a similar role to latent intents in VLAs — they compress temporally extended behavior into a compact bottleneck. The difference is that HWM's bottleneck is explicitly designed for planning (optimized via CEM) rather than policy learning. This suggests a convergence point: latent intent bottlenecks that are simultaneously useful for planning and policy execution.

**inverse-dynamics-head-world-model-policy.md**: HWM notably does *not* use an inverse dynamics model to translate subgoals into actions. Instead, it uses forward model-based MPC at the low level to reach subgoals. This avoids the inverse dynamics problem entirely but requires the subgoal to lie in a reachable region of the latent space. The dimensionality ablation shows this constraint is real — too-expressive high-level actions produce unreachable subgoals.

### Connection to Angles

**how-do-video-world-models-handle-object-permanence.md** (high priority): HWM's hierarchical structure has interesting implications for object permanence. The high-level world model operates at a coarser temporal scale and may naturally maintain coarse object representations across occlusion events (contact events, motion direction preserved despite reduced visual precision — see §4.3). The finding that "lower-dimensional latent spaces yield noisier predictions but preserve coarse semantic structure" suggests that the high-level model might handle object permanence better precisely because it operates at a coarser abstraction level. This could be tested by evaluating HWM on tasks requiring object permanence reasoning.

**can-rl-post-training-replace-architectural-complexity-in-world-models.md**: HWM provides a clear answer: **architectural hierarchy (inference-time) provides benefits that post-training RL alone cannot match**. The RL baselines (GCIQL, HIQL, HILP) all degrade sharply at longer horizons, while HWM maintains robust performance. The hierarchical planning abstraction is orthogonal to and complementary with RL — you could potentially combine HWM's planning with RL-trained world models for even better performance. The paper demonstrates that adding hierarchical planning as a plug-in to existing pretrained WMs is more effective than training with more data (77× more data for VLAs still underperforms HWM on non-greedy tasks).
