## problem

Offline trajectory optimization when system dynamics $f$ are **completely unknown** and no surrogate model can be simulated. An agent has access only to a static dataset $\mathcal{D} = \{\tau_i\}_{i=1}^N$ of horizon-$H$ trajectories collected by heterogeneous, suboptimal controllers (10% LQR with noise, 10% noisy PD, 80% random open-loop).

The core objective is:

$$\min_{\mu \in \Delta(\mathcal{T}_H(x))} \mathbb{E}_{\tau \sim \mu}[J_x(\tau; \omega)] \quad \text{s.t.} \quad \mu(\mathrm{d}x_{t+1} \mid \tau_t) = f(\mathrm{d}x_{t+1} \mid x_t, u_t)$$

where $J_x(\tau; \omega) = \sum_{t=0}^{H-1}(x_t^\top Q(\omega) x_t + u_t^\top R(\omega) u_t) + x_H^\top Q(\omega) x_H$ is a quadratic cost parameterized by $\omega = (q, r) \in \Omega$.

**Key constraints**: no model rollouts, no online interaction. The agent must learn a *conditional distribution* over full trajectories, not just actions. Standard distribution matching (e.g., vanilla drifting or behavior cloning) only recovers the offline behavior prior $P_\mathcal{D}$, not cost-optimal plans.

**Benchmark system**: 1D mass-spring-damper with $m=1.0$, $k_s=1.0$, $c=0.2$, $\Delta t=0.05$, discretized exactly under zero-order hold. Planning horizons $H \in \{30, 50, 100\}$. Initial states sampled from $[-2, 2] \times [-2, 2]$.

## architecture

**Drifting MPC** combines drifting generative models with receding-horizon planning via exponentially tilted priors.

### Generator

A conditional pushforward generator $G_\theta: \mathbb{R}^{d_\epsilon} \times \mathbb{R}^{2d_x + d_u} \to \mathbb{R}^{H \cdot (d_x + d_u) + d_x}$ maps noise $\epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I)$ and a query $c = (x_0, \omega)$ to a full trajectory $\tau = G_\theta(\epsilon, c)$ in a **single forward pass**. This induces the conditional pushforward distribution $q_\theta(\cdot \mid c) = [G_\theta(\cdot, c)]_\# p_\epsilon$.

### Exponentially Tilted Target

Instead of matching the raw offline prior $p_0$, the target distribution is:

$$p_\beta(\mathrm{d}\tau \mid c) \propto \exp(-\beta J_{x_0}(\tau; \omega)) \, p_0(\mathrm{d}\tau \mid x_0)$$

**Theorem 1** proves $p_\beta$ is the unique minimizer of $\mathbb{E}_p[J_x] + \frac{1}{\beta} \mathrm{KL}(p \| p_0)$, trading off cost minimization with proximity to the offline prior.

### Cost-Aware Drift Field

The key innovation is the **Tilting Lemma** (Lemma 1): rather than sampling from the globally normalized tilted distribution, the positive drift field is modified with importance weights:

$$V_{p_0}^+(\tau; \beta, c) = \frac{\mathbb{E}_{\tau' \sim p_0} \left[ w_\beta(\tau'; c) \, k(\tau, \tau') (\tau' - \tau) \right]}{\mathbb{E}_{\tau' \sim p_0} \left[ w_\beta(\tau'; c) \, k(\tau, \tau') \right]}$$

where $w_\beta(\tau; c) = \exp(-\beta J_{x_0}(\tau; \omega))$ and $k(\tau, \tau') = \exp\left(-\frac{\|\tau - \tau'\|_2^2}{T}\right)$ is a Gaussian kernel with temperature $T$.

### Local Empirical Prior

Positive samples are drawn from a local empirical prior conditioned on the current state $x_0$:

$$\hat{p}_0(\cdot \mid x_0) = \sum_{i=1}^{N} \alpha_i(x_0) \delta_{\tau_i}(\cdot), \quad \alpha_i(x_0) \propto k_x(x_0, x_i) \cdot \mathbf{1}\{i \in \mathcal{N}_K(x_0)\}$$

where $\mathcal{N}_K(x_0)$ retrieves the $K$ nearest neighbors from $\mathcal{D}$ by initial-state distance.

### Best-of-$M$ Receding-Horizon Inference

At test time, given state $x$ and cost parameter $\omega$:
1. Sample $M_{\mathrm{plan}}$ candidate trajectories: $\tau^{(m)} = G_\theta(\epsilon_m, c)$, $\epsilon_m \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I)$
2. Evaluate cost $C_m = J_x(\tau^{(m)}; \omega)$ for each
3. Select $m^\star = \arg\min_m C_m$
4. Execute first control of $\tau^{(m^\star)}$, observe next state, repeat

**Theorem 2** provides a best-of-$M$ guarantee: $\mathbb{P}(\bigcup_{t=0}^{T-1} E_{\delta, t}) \leq T \exp(-M_{\mathrm{plan}} \max(0, \eta(\omega) - \varepsilon(\omega)))$, where $\eta$ measures the tilted target's coverage of $\delta$-optimal trajectories and $\varepsilon$ measures the generator's TV distance to $p_\beta$.

## training

The training objective averages the conditional drift loss over random planning queries:

$$\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{train}}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{x_0 \sim \hat{\rho}_0, \, \omega \sim \mathrm{Unif}(\Omega)} \left[ \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{drift}}(\theta; (x_0, \omega)) \right]$$

where the per-query fixed-point drift loss is:

$$\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{drift}}(\theta; c) = \mathbb{E}\left[ \left\| \tau - \operatorname{sg}\!\left(\tau + \widehat{V}_{\hat{p}_0, q_\theta}(\tau; \beta, c)\right) \right\|_2^2 \right]$$

with $\operatorname{sg}(\cdot)$ denoting stop-gradient, and the empirical drift field $\widehat{V}_{\hat{p}_0, q_\theta} = \widehat{V}_{\hat{p}_0}^+ - \widehat{V}_{q_\theta}^-$.

**Training procedure** (Algorithm 1):
- Sample queries $c_b = (x_0^{(b)}, \omega^{(b)})$ with $x_0^{(b)} \sim \hat{\rho}_0$, $\omega^{(b)} \sim \mathrm{Unif}(\Omega)$
- For each query: retrieve $K$ nearest-neighbor positive trajectories, compute their relabeled costs $\tilde{J}_i(\omega)$, sample $M$ negative trajectories from the current generator
- Compute cost-weighted positive drift and negative repulsion drift using minibatch kernel estimates
- Update $\theta$ by SGD on the averaged drift loss

**Key design choices**:
- $\beta$ is scheduled (increased during training): starts small to match the behavior prior for stability, then grows to sharpen toward cost-optimal trajectories
- Normalized positive weights: $\tilde{k}^+_{\mathcal{B},\beta}(\tau, \tau^+; c) = \frac{e^{-\beta \tilde{J}_i(\omega)} k(\tau, \tau^+)}{\sum_{\tau_j \in \mathcal{B}^+} e^{-\beta \tilde{J}_j(\omega)} k(\tau, \tau_j)}$
- All methods trained for **500 epochs** on the same dataset

## evaluation

Evaluated on the 1D mass-spring-damper system across three planning horizons. Oracle is finite-horizon LQR via backward Riccati recursion. **100 rollouts** per method, cost statistics via BCa bootstrap with 10,000 resamples.

| Method | Avg Cost (H=30) | Avg Cost (H=50) | Avg Cost (H=100) | Time ms (H=30) | Time ms (H=50) | Time ms (H=100) |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Oracle** | 90.1 ± 12.9 | 107.3 ± 13.6 | 93.2 ± 9.5 | 13.3 ± 0.1 | 36.4 ± 0.2 | 141 ± 0.4 |
| **Drift MPC** | **105.8 ± 14.2** | **131.5 ± 15.6** | **122.6 ± 11.3** | **29.8 ± 0.9** | **54.3 ± 0.7** | **135 ± 0.8** |
| Drift Prior | 116.9 ± 15.6 | 169.2 ± 21.4 | 168.1 ± 16.1 | 28.5 ± 0.2 | 52.4 ± 0.2 | 134 ± 0.5 |
| Diffusion | 119.6 ± 16.2 | 201.9 ± 22.7 | 8647 ± 694 | 1623 ± 3.3 | 2707 ± 4.6 | 5514 ± 8.5 |
| Guided Diffusion | 106.4 ± 14.6 | 623.5 ± 201.5 | 27317 ± 6192 | 2068 ± 3.7 | 3467 ± 5.9 | 7055 ± 10.8 |

**Key results**:
- **Near-oracle cost**: Drift MPC achieves 17% gap from oracle at $H=30$, 23% at $H=50$, 32% at $H=100$ — consistently the closest to oracle among all baselines
- **Speed advantage over diffusion**: At $H=100$, Drift MPC is **40× faster** than Diffusion (135 ms vs. 5514 ms) and **52× faster** than Guided Diffusion (135 ms vs. 7055 ms), with dramatically better cost
- **Guided Diffusion degrades catastrophically** at longer horizons: avg cost 27,317 at $H=100$ vs. Drift MPC's 122.6
- **Drift Prior** (no cost conditioning) underperforms Drift MPC by 8–30%, validating the importance of the tilted drift
- Drift MPC inference time is within **2× of the oracle** (29.8 ms vs. 13.3 ms at $H=30$), making it practical for real-time receding-horizon control
- Median costs follow the same ranking: Drift MPC 54.3, Drift Prior 60.1, Diffusion 58.2, Guided Diffusion 51.4 (at $H=30$)

Diffusion methods use 64 denoising steps at inference.

- Kernel temperature $T$ for trajectory similarity

**Evaluation**:
- 100 rollout episodes per method per horizon
- BCa bootstrap with 10,000 resamples for confidence intervals
- Report mean ± SE and median [IQR] for costs and timing

## notes

- The paper establishes a clean **control-as-inference** connection: the tilted distribution $p_\beta$ is exactly the solution to a KL-regularized optimal control problem (Theorem 1), bridging drifting generative models and regularized MPC theory.
- The **Tilting Lemma** (Lemma 1) is the key practical insight — it shows that only minibatch-level importance weights $e^{-\beta J}$ are needed, not samples from the globally normalized target. This makes the algorithm tractable.
- The $\beta$ scheduling strategy is critical: small $\beta$ early acts as behavior cloning for stability, large $\beta$ later sharpens toward optimality. The paper does not specify the exact schedule, which would be important for reproduction.
- The **single-step inference** property is the main selling point vs. diffusion planners. At $H=100$, Drift MPC achieves comparable cost to oracle (122.6 vs. 93.2) in nearly the same time (135 ms vs. 141 ms), while diffusion methods are 40–50× slower with catastrophically worse cost.
- Limitations acknowledged: only tested on a 1D linear system; extensions to higher-dimensional and nonlinear systems are left as future work. The paper explicitly calls for "broader investigation of alternative guidance mechanisms."
- The $M_{\mathrm{plan}}$ value used at test time is not explicitly stated in the text — this is a notable omission for reproducibility.
- No explicit mention of network architecture details (layers, width, activation) or optimizer settings (learning rate, scheduler).
