## problem

Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models with diffusion-based action heads rely on **action chunking** — executing a sequence of $h$ actions per inference step without intermediate replanning — to reduce compounding errors and improve temporal consistency. However, the chunk size $h$ creates a fundamental tradeoff:

- **Large $h$**: good temporal consistency but poor reactivity to new observations, leading to suboptimal corrections during critical manipulation phases.
- **Small $h$**: high reactivity but prone to **mode-jumping** — jerky, discontinuous behavior at chunk boundaries.

Current practice uses a **fixed chunk size** selected empirically per benchmark, which is suboptimal because:
1. The optimal $h$ varies across tasks (e.g., pick-and-place vs. button pressing vs. long-horizon manipulation).
2. The optimal $h$ varies across backbones (GR00T N1.5 vs. $\pi\_0$ vs. SmolVLA).
3. Even within a single episode, different phases demand different chunk sizes (approach vs. grasp vs. transport).

The paper demonstrates empirically that on RoboCasa Kitchen with GR00T N1.5, task success rates are **highly sensitive** to chunk size, and no single fixed $h$ is optimal across 24 tasks. Fixed-size methods like ACT (EMA smoothing), BID (backward coherence search), and TV-BID (TVD-based adaptation) all use a fixed $h$ throughout an entire episode.

## architecture

AAC is a **training-free, architecture-agnostic inference-time strategy** — it requires no modifications to the VLA model itself. The approach works with any diffusion-based or flow-matching VLA action head (GR00T N1.5, $\pi\_{0.5}$, etc.).

**Core idea**: Use **action entropy** as a signal to dynamically determine the optimal chunk size $h^*$ at each inference timestep.

**Action entropy computation** (for a 7-DOF arm + gripper):
- **Continuous controls** (3-DOF translation $[\Delta x, \Delta y, \Delta z]$, 3-DOF rotation $[\Delta R\_x, \Delta R\_y, \Delta R\_z]$): Gaussian differential entropy

$$E^i\_t = \frac{1}{2}\log\left[(2\pi e)^d \det(\Sigma\_t)\right]$$

- **Discrete controls** (gripper $G \in \{0,1\}$): Shannon entropy

$$E^i\_g = -\sum_{a \in \mathcal{A}} p(a)\log(p(a))$$

To estimate $p(a)$ and $\Sigma\_t$, $N=20$ candidate action chunks are sampled in parallel via batched inference. Gripper probability is estimated as $p(a) = c/N$ where $c$ is the count of close-state predictions.

**Average action entropy** for chunk size $h$:

$$\overline{E}\_h = \frac{1}{h}\sum_{i=t}^{t+h-1}\sum_{j \in \{t,r,g\}} E^i\_j$$

**Optimal chunk size** selection:

$$h^* = \max\left(\arg\max\_h (\overline{E}\_{h+1} - \overline{E}\_h),\ \xi\right)$$

where:
- $\arg\max\_h (\overline{E}\_{h+1} - \overline{E}\_h)$ finds the **maximum differential point** — the step where adding one more action to the chunk causes the largest entropy jump (signaling deteriorating prediction quality).
- $\xi$ is a **minimum action magnitude** constraint ensuring sufficient movement energy ($\alpha=3$), computed as the smallest $l$ such that $m(l) > \alpha$, where $m(l)$ sums translation Euclidean norm, accumulated quaternion rotation norm, and gripper switch indicator.

**Qualitative behavior**: AAC naturally learns to use large chunks during coarse approach/transport phases (low entropy, high confidence) and small chunks during precise grasping/manipulation phases (high entropy, needs frequent replanning).

## training

AAC requires **no additional training** — it operates purely at inference time. The underlying VLA model uses standard training:

**Simulation (RoboCasa):**
- Base: Public GR00T N1.5 checkpoint (Eagle-2 VLM backbone + flow-matching DiT action head)
- Fine-tuning: 100 MimicGen-generated demo trajectories per task, 90 epochs
- Hardware: 8 × NVIDIA A800 GPUs, mixed-precision training
- Frozen: Eagle-2 VLM backbone and vision projector
- Fine-tuned: DiT backbone, state encoder/decoder, action encoder/decoder

**Simulation (LIBERO):**
- All 4 task suites (40 tasks) jointly trained for 90 epochs
- 50 human-teleoperated demonstrations per task (pre-processed by OpenVLA/$\pi\_0$)

**Real-world:**
- 50 human-teleoperated demonstrations per task (SpaceMouse teleoperation)
- Fine-tuned on 8 × NVIDIA A800 GPUs for 300 epochs, batch size 32 per GPU
- Robot: Realman single-arm with Mycobot gripper
- Observations: wrist RGB + side RGB (tripod)

**AAC inference cost:** $N=20$ parallel action chunk samples (default), adding ~23ms latency on a single A800 GPU (106ms total vs. 83ms baseline). With $N \leq 10$, overhead is negligible ($\leq 1.3$ms).

## reproduction guide

1. **Environment**: Python environment with GR00T N1.5 (NVIDIA cosmos repo) or $\pi\_{0.5}$ installed.
2. **Baselines**: Use pre-trained GR00T N1.5 checkpoint; fine-tune diffusion head only on target benchmarks.
3. **RoboCasa setup**:
   - Fine-tune on 100 MimicGen trajectories/task for 90 epochs on 8 × A800 GPUs.
   - Evaluate with 100 rollouts per task.
4. **LIBERO setup**:
   - Joint training on all 4 suites (40 tasks), 90 epochs.
   - Evaluate with 50 rollouts per task.
5. **AAC integration**:
   - At each inference step, generate $N=20$ action chunk candidates via batched parallel sampling.
   - Compute continuous entropy (Eq. 3) from sample covariance, discrete entropy (Eq. 2) from sample frequencies.
   - Find optimal chunk size $h^*$ via maximum entropy differential (Eq. 5) with minimum magnitude constraint $\xi$ ($\alpha=3$).
   - Execute first $h^*$ actions, then replan.
6. **Code**: Project page with source code at [lance-lot.github.io/adaptive-chunking.github.io](https://lance-lot.github.io/adaptive-chunking.github.io/).
7. **Compute**: Main experiments run on 8 × NVIDIA A800 GPUs; inference overhead with $N=20$ samples is ~23ms on a single A800.

## notes

- **Training-free**: AAC's key strength is that it requires zero additional training or architectural changes — it is a pure inference-time strategy that wraps around any diffusion/flow-matching VLA.
- **Entropy as uncertainty signal**: The insight that entropy differential $\overline{E}\_{h+1} - \overline{E}\_h$ reveals when extending the action chunk degrades quality is simple but effective.
- **Practical tradeoff**: With $N=20$ samples, inference latency increases by ~28% (83→106ms) for a 0.9pp gain on LIBERO. The paper argues this is acceptable given the parallel computation is batched.
- **Real-world gains are substantial**: +15pp average improvement on 3 real-world tasks is the strongest result, suggesting AAC's adaptive behavior is particularly valuable under real-world distribution shift and noise.
- **Limitations**: No single fixed-$h$ is always beaten per-task (e.g., AAC achieves 62.0 on RoboCasa while fixed h=8 gets 61.2 — AAC wins on average but some individual tasks see slight regressions like Counter to Stove: 41.0→40.0).
- **CVPR 2026 acceptance** signals growing interest in inference-time compute strategies for embodied AI.
- **Applicable beyond GR00T**: Results on $\pi\_{0.5}$ confirm generalization to other flow-matching VLA architectures, though adaptation to non-diffusion VLAs (e.g., discretized token-prediction models like OpenVLA/RT-2) is not demonstrated.
