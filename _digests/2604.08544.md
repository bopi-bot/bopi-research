## problem

Deformable object manipulation---particularly garment folding---is among the most data-hungry regimes in embodied learning. Shape, contact, and topology co-evolve in ways that far exceed the variability of rigid objects. While data scaling consistently improves VLA performance (Black et al., 2024; Bjorck et al., 2025; Cai et al., 2026), acquiring real-world deformable interaction data at scale remains prohibitively expensive: it demands skilled operators, specialized hardware, and extensive human labor.

Simulation-to-real (S2R) synthetic data generation has emerged as a scaling strategy, but **existing S2R pipelines are rooted in rigid-body abstractions** and break down in three ways for deformable manipulation:

1. **Geometric misalignment**: Simulated scenes are weakly aligned or unaligned with real environments, relying on coarse calibration or manually constructed assets (e.g., Gr00topia, Wang et al., 2024; GenManip, Gao et al.). Such inconsistencies are amplified under soft-body deformation.

2. **Unstable soft-body dynamics**: Physics engines (FEM, VBD, PBD) are optimized for rigid-body dynamics, producing unrealistic stretching, particle gaps, and spiky deformations during cloth-robot contact (Chen et al., 2025a; Zhang et al., 2024).

3. **Inadequate motion primitives**: Behavior generation follows rigid-object paradigms (grasp-point, pick-and-place) via methods like MimicGen (Mandlekar et al., 2023), RoboCasa (Nasiriany et al., 2026), RobotWin 2.0 (Chen et al., 2025a), and DexMimicGen (Jiang et al., 2025). Naive trajectory slicing destroys interaction fidelity for state-dependent, contact-rich deformable tasks.

The core insight: **simulation fails not for being synthetic, but for being ungrounded**. SIM1 posits that real-to-sim (R2S) alignment is foundational---scaling becomes valuable only once simulated physics correspond to reality.

**Prior methods explicitly referenced and superseded:**
- **MimicGen** (Mandlekar et al., 2023): rigid-body trajectory slicing/recomposition---0% pass rate on deformable tasks
- **RoboCasa365** (Nasiriany et al., 2026): rigid-scenario sim framework
- **Genie Sim 3.0** (Yin et al.): humanoid sim platform, rigid-body focused
- **ManiSkill2** (Gu et al., 2023): rigid manipulation benchmark
- **GarmentLab** (Lu et al., 2024): multi-engine garment platform, solver accuracy limited
- **VBD** (Chen et al., 2024a): suffers unrealistic stretching under rigid-soft contact
- **VIRAL** (He et al., 2025): S2R for humanoid loco-manipulation
- **InternData-A1** (Tian et al., 2025): high-fidelity synthetic data for generalist policy pretraining
- **Gaussian-splatting demo gen** (Yang et al., 2025): single-shot manipulation via novel views

## architecture

SIM1 implements a **real-to-sim-to-real (R2S2R)** paradigm across three alignment stages: geometric (scene digitization), dynamical (physics simulation), and movement (trajectory generation).

### SIM1-Scene: Real Scene Digitization (Section 3.1)

**Deformable assets.** Garments are captured with an **EinScan Rigil Pro** professional 3D scanner mounted on a mannequin. Multi-view RGB + LiDAR scans are fused into a dense point cloud, mannequin points are manually segmented, and the garment cloud undergoes:
- Poisson surface reconstruction (Kazhdan & Hoppe, 2013)
- Hole filling, smoothing, remeshing into watertight meshes
- RGB texture mapping onto static OBJ assets

Result: **sub-millimeter geometric replicas** of real garments. Marker-based methods (e.g., AR Code) produce only centimeter-level meshes with noticeable artifacts.

**Robot assets.** ARX ACONE bimanual platform---URDF imported directly from manufacturer CAD models (SolidWorks). Joint limits, collision geometries, and visual meshes match real hardware exactly. Arms calibrated relative to each other for correct bimanual coordination.

**Environment assets.** Tables from 3D model repositories, scaled per real-world measurements, placed at metric-accurate positions and orientations.

### SIM1-Sim: Deformation-Stable Physics Simulation (Section 3.2)

The solver extends the **Augmented Vertex Block Descent (AVBD)** formulation (Giles et al., SIGGRAPH 2025), built on Newton--VBD (Chen et al., 2024a). It operates on a triangular mesh where edges $\mathbf{e} = (\mathbf{e}\_{i}, \mathbf{e}\_{j})$ connect vertices.

**StVK elasticity model.** The St. Venant--Kirchhoff hyperelastic model (Kikuuwe et al., 2009) assigns energy to how much each triangle deforms from its rest shape. With deformation gradient $\mathbf{F}$ and Green-Lagrange strain $\mathbf{G} = \frac{1}{2}(\mathbf{F}^{T}\mathbf{F} - \mathbf{I})$, the elastic energy density is:

$$\Psi(\mathbf{e}) = \mu\|\mathbf{G}\|_{F}^{2} + \frac{1}{2}\hat{\lambda}(\text{tr}(\mathbf{G}))^{2}$$

where $\mu$ and $\hat{\lambda}$ are Lamé parameters controlling shear and area-change resistance. The stretching force is $\mathbf{f}\_{i}^{\text{stretch}} = -\partial\Psi / \partial\mathbf{e}\_{i}$.

**Dihedral-angle bending model** (Zhou et al., 2008). For four vertices $\mathbf{e}\_{i}, \mathbf{e}\_{j}, \mathbf{e}\_{k}, \mathbf{e}\_{l}$ sharing an edge with rest angle $\theta\_{0}$:

$$E\_{\text{bend}} = k\_{\text{bend}} \, l\_{\text{edge}} \, (\theta(\mathbf{e}\_{i}, \mathbf{e}\_{j}, \mathbf{e}\_{k}, \mathbf{e}\_{l}) - \theta\_{0})^{2}$$

**Strain constraint (key contribution).** A maximum stretch ratio $\xi > 0$ is enforced. The constraint activates when edge length exceeds $(1+\xi)l\_{0}$:

$$C(\mathbf{e}) = \|\mathbf{e}\_{i} - \mathbf{e}\_{j}\| - (1+\xi)l\_{0} \leq 0$$

When $C(\mathbf{e}) > 0$, a virtual elastic energy is injected:

$$E\_{\text{strain}}^{(n)}(\mathbf{e}) = \begin{cases} \frac{1}{2}k^{(n)}C(\mathbf{e})^{2} + \lambda^{(n)}C(\mathbf{e}), & C(\mathbf{e}) > 0, \\ 0, & \text{otherwise}, \end{cases}$$

where $k^{(n)}$ is penalty stiffness and $\lambda^{(n)}$ is the Lagrange multiplier. Parameters update iteratively:

$$k^{(n+1)} = \min(k\_{\max},\; k^{(n)} + \beta\lvert C(\mathbf{e}) \rvert), \quad \lambda^{(n+1)} = \lambda^{(n)} + k^{(n)}C(\mathbf{e})$$

This acts as a virtual spring that activates only during excessive stretching, injecting corrective forces that guide vertices toward physically consistent configurations.

**Total force assembly:**

$$\mathbf{f}\_{i}^{\text{total}} = \mathbf{f}\_{i}^{\text{stretch}} + \mathbf{f}\_{i}^{\text{bend}} + \mathbf{f}\_{i}^{\text{strain}} + \mathbf{f}\_{i}^{\text{ext}}$$

Vertex displacement is solved via a local $3 \times 3$ Newton system:

$$\Delta\mathbf{e}\_{i} = \mathbf{A}\_{i}^{-1}\mathbf{b}\_{i}$$

where $\mathbf{A}\_{i} = \frac{M\_{i}}{\Delta t^{2}}\mathbf{I} + \sum\mathbf{H}\_{i}$ and $\mathbf{b}\_{i} = \mathbf{f}\_{i}^{\text{total}} - \frac{M\_{i}}{\Delta t^{2}}(\mathbf{e}\_{i}^{(n)} - \hat{\mathbf{e}}\_{i})$.

**Penetration avoidance.** A geometric safety filter clips displacements: $s = \min(1, d\_{\text{safe}} / \|\Delta\mathbf{e}\_{i}\|)$ where $d\_{\text{safe}} = \beta \cdot \min(r\_{\text{collision}}, d\_{\text{tri}}^{\min}, d\_{\text{edge}}^{\min})$ with $\beta = 0.42$.

**Parameter calibration infrastructure.** Physical parameters $\Theta = \{\rho, E, \nu, \mu, \eta, \zeta\}$ (density, Young's modulus, Poisson's ratio, friction, restitution, relaxation) are calibrated via **visual behavior matching**: an expert operates the real dual-arm robot while joint states stream to the simulator. Renderings are visually compared with real executions; parameters are iteratively adjusted until draping, folding, and contact behaviors match. This establishes behavioral consistency rather than recovering true physical parameters.

**GPU-accelerated simulation.** Powered by **NVIDIA Warp** (Python → native CUDA kernels), running rigid-body dynamics and AVBD cloth solver entirely on GPU within unified memory at **~15 fps**. Data recorded asynchronously: robot states in NPZ format, full trajectories in USD format.

### SIM1-DataGen: Structured Deformable Manipulation Synthesis (Section 3.3)

**Trajectory decomposition.** Demonstrations $\mathcal{D} = \{\tau\_{i}\}$ are segmented into **interacting** (stable grasp configurations, contact states) and **moving** (transitions) regions. Interacting segments are pooled without modification; for new demonstrations, segments are randomly sampled and reordered while preserving grasp poses.

**Diffusion-based motion generation.** Between consecutive interacting segments, motion is synthesized via **conditional diffusion forcing** (Chen et al., NeurIPS 2024). Given boundary poses $(\mathbf{p}\_{s}, \mathbf{p}\_{e})$ and robot history $\mathbf{h}$, a transformer sequence model predicts noise residuals conditioned on history and keyposes:

$$\min\_{\theta}\; \mathbb{E}\|\boldsymbol{\epsilon} - \epsilon\_{\theta}(g(\mathbf{x}^{0}, \mathbf{k}); \mathbf{h}, \mathbf{p}\_{s}, \mathbf{p}\_{e}, \mathbf{k})\|_{2}^{2}$$

where $g$ is a corruption function applying noise level $\mathbf{k} \in (0,1]^{\mathcal{T}}$ and $\epsilon\_{\theta}$ is a transformer noise predictor.

**Validity checking (two-stage).**
1. **Lightweight state-based filtering**: particle statistics from simulation define admissible regions via threshold rules (synthesized via vibe coding), pruning invalid samples early.
2. **Binary video discriminator $D$**: ResNet-18 feature extractor + Transformer encoder on head-view RGB observations $\mathbf{V}$ outputs validity score $s = D(\mathbf{V})$. Trajectories with $s > \tau\_{\text{disc}}$ are retained. Negative cases arise naturally from simulation. The discriminator achieves **>99% filtering success**.

**Visual randomization.** Valid trajectories rendered in **Blender** with randomized materials (17 table types, 28 cloth textures), lighting, and camera parameters. Multiple appearance variations per trajectory using cycle path tracing for photorealistic RGB. Final dataset in **LeRobot format** (Cadene et al., 2024).

## training

**Hardware.**
- Real robot: ARX ACONE dual-arm platform with parallel-jaw grippers
- Teleoperation: ARX X5 arms controlling simulated ACONE in real time
- Simulation workstation: NVIDIA RTX 4090 GPU
- Rendering server: $8 \times$ NVIDIA RTX 4090 GPUs

**Data collection.**
- Real world: 1,000 trajectories via kinesthetic teaching (operator guides end-effectors by hand)
- Simulation: 200 source demonstrations via visual teleoperation
- Synthetic generation: 200 seeds scaled to 10,000 samples (in-domain) / 2,000 (out-of-domain)
- Randomization: garment translated within 5 cm, rotated $\pm 15^{\circ}$; 17 table materials, 28 cloth textures; 90 environment combinations; head-camera elevation $\pm 5^{\circ}$

**Policy architectures.**
- **$\pi\_{0.5}$** (Black et al., 2024; Intelligence et al., 2025): vision-language-action flow model with open-world generalization, post-trained or from scratch
- **$\pi\_{0}$** (Black et al., 2024): vision-language-action flow model for general robot control, post-trained

Both are standard open-source VLA policies. The paper does not modify policy architectures; it focuses entirely on the data generation pipeline.

**Diffusion model for trajectory generation.**
- Conditional diffusion forcing (Chen et al., NeurIPS 2024)
- Transformer sequence model for noise prediction
- Trained on 200 teleoperated demonstrations in simulation

**Discriminator for validity checking.**
- ResNet-18 backbone + Transformer encoder for temporal aggregation
- Trained on positive (successful) and negative (naturally arising failure) simulation trajectories
- Binary classification: validity score threshold $\tau\_{\text{disc}}$

**Evaluation protocol.** 30 trials per configuration. Success = garment reaches target folded configuration without dropping or unfolding.

## evaluation

### Main Results (Q1--Q3)

**Simulation vs. real-data parity (Q1).** Under equal data budgets (200 samples each):

| Training Data | $\pi\_{0.5}$ Success |
|---|---|
| Real demonstrations | **97%** |
| Sim-teleoperated data | **87%** |
| Gap | 10% |

This 10% gap confirms physics-aligned simulation provides supervision of considerable fidelity.

**Out-of-domain robustness (Q2).** Simulation-trained policies outperform real-data baselines under domain shifts:

| Domain Shift | Sim Gain over Real |
|---|---|
| Spatial (up to 8 cm translation, $\pm 15^{\circ}$ rotation) | **+50%** |
| Texture (unseen table/garment textures + friction) | **+13%** |
| Lighting (randomized direction/intensity) | **+47%** |

**Pretraining confound analysis.** $\pi\_{0.5}$ from scratch with real data: **0%** success. With synthetic data: **76%** success. This dispels the possibility that task success is driven by pretrained priors.

**Scaling analysis (Q3).** For $\pi\_{0.5}$ in-domain:
- **1:15 equivalence ratio**: one real demonstration provides comparable benefit to ~15 synthetic samples at saturation
- For out-of-domain texture generalization: shifts to **1:5** ratio (synthetic is more efficient per sample)
- Synthetic data is weak in extremely low-data regimes but scales more effectively; real-data gains saturate due to limited diversity

**Zero-shot transfer to unseen garment.** On a polo shirt with different material, texture, and geometry (absent from training): real-data baseline achieves **20%**, SIM1 synthetic achieves **70%**.

## reproduction guide

**Code and data are publicly available:**
- Code: [github.com/InternRobotics/SIM1](https://github.com/InternRobotics/SIM1)
- Dataset: [huggingface.co/datasets/InternRobotics/Sim1-Datset](https://huggingface.co/datasets/InternRobotics/Sim1-Datset)
- Homepage: [internrobotics.github.io/sim1.github.io/](https://internrobotics.github.io/sim1.github.io/)
- Demo: [sim1-demo.intern-robotics.com](https://sim1-demo.intern-robotics.com/)

**Step-by-step reproduction:**

1. **Scene digitization**: Scan target garment with a high-precision 3D scanner (EinScan Rigil Pro or equivalent). Process through Poisson reconstruction, hole filling, and remeshing. Import robot URDF and environment assets with metric-accurate placement.

2. **Simulation setup**: Install NVIDIA Warp. Configure the AVBD solver with calibrated parameters from Table 2. Set up bidirectional teleoperation: map real robot joint states to simulator via $\mathbf{q}\_{\text{sim}}(t)[\mathcal{I}\_{\text{sim}}] = \mathbf{q}\_{\text{real}}(t)[\mathcal{I}\_{\text{arm}}]$. Gripper mapping: $o\_{L} = -\text{clip}(q\_{\text{finger},L} / (2 \cdot q\_{\text{max}}), -1, 0)$ where $q\_{\text{max}} = 1.62$ rad.

3. **Teleoperation data collection**: Use the simulation infrastructure (~15 fps) to collect ~200 demonstrations via visual teleoperation.

4. **Trajectory synthesis**: Decompose demonstrations into interacting/moving segments. Train diffusion model (conditional diffusion forcing transformer). Generate trajectories using Algorithm 1 (Section C). Filter via particle-state rules + video discriminator (ResNet-18 + Transformer).

5. **Rendering**: Render accepted trajectories in Blender with appearance randomization (materials, lighting, camera). Export in LeRobot format.

6. **Policy training**: Train $\pi\_{0.5}$ or $\pi\_{0}$ on synthetic dataset. Evaluate on real robot with 30 trials per configuration.

**Key dependencies:**
- NVIDIA Warp (GPU-accelerated physics)
- Blender (photorealistic rendering with cycle path tracing)
- LeRobot (data format for imitation learning)
- ARX ACONE robot platform (or equivalent bimanual setup with URDF)
- $\pi\_{0.5}$ / $\pi\_{0}$ policy implementations

**Compute requirements:**
- Simulation + data generation: 1× RTX 4090 workstation
- Large-scale rendering: 8× RTX 4090 server (710 trajectories/day, 16.2 min per GPU)
- Policy training: standard GPU for VLA fine-tuning

**Limitation**: Material calibration requires expert-guided parameter tuning per garment type. Full automation across arbitrary cloth types remains an open problem.

## notes

**Synthetic data as a bridge across the cross-embodiment gap.** SIM1's most striking result is the **93% zero-shot polo-shirt folding success**---a garment with substantially different geometry, size, material, and friction from anything in the training distribution. This suggests that physics-aligned simulation captures *interaction priors* that transfer across embodiments and objects, not just visual appearance. The simulation learns "how cloth responds to manipulation" at a physical level, which generalizes better than memorizing specific visual trajectories.

**World models as supervision engines.** SIM1 reframes simulation not as a surrogate environment for RL, but as a **structured supervision engine** for imitation learning. The physics simulator acts as a world model that enforces physical plausibility (via the AVBD solver), while the diffusion-based trajectory generator provides behavioral diversity. The discriminator serves as a learned physics validator. This three-component architecture (world model + behavior generator + quality filter) is a pattern that could generalize beyond cloth manipulation to any domain where physics constrains valid behavior.

**The 1:15 ratio is task-dependent, not fundamental.** The equivalence ratio shifts from 15:1 (in-domain) to 5:1 (out-of-domain texture), suggesting synthetic data is *more* efficient for generalization. This inverts the common assumption that real data is inherently higher quality---for coverage of variation, simulation may be superior.

**Connection to data-efficient policy learning for Binh's interests:**
- SIM1 demonstrates that ~200 expert demonstrations can seed a pipeline generating 10k+ training-quality samples
- The R2S2R paradigm could extend to other deformable domains: surgical manipulation, food handling, cable routing
- The AVBD solver's strain constraint mechanism is a general technique for stabilizing any rigid-soft coupling simulation
- The visual randomization pipeline (28 cloth textures, 17 table materials, 90 environments) is directly applicable to domain randomization for robust policy learning
- The cost analysis ($0.10 vs. $2.71 per trajectory) makes large-scale deformable manipulation research accessible without massive robot infrastructure

**Open questions for further research:**
- Can the expert calibration step be replaced with automated parameter optimization (e.g., differentiable simulation)?
- How does the pipeline extend to dynamic garments (e.g., wearable dressing tasks)?
- Can the diffusion-based trajectory generator be conditioned on language instructions for task diversity?
- What is the minimum number of source demonstrations needed for reliable data generation?
