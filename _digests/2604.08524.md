## Problem

Steering vectors applied to LLM hidden activations are an efficient alignment technique (Zou et al., 2025; Turner et al., 2023) but we lack a mechanistic explanation of *how* they work -- specifically which internal components they affect and how this produces behavioral shifts. This paper conducts a comprehensive mechanistic case study on **refusal steering** (Arditi et al., 2024) to understand the causal pathways through which steering vectors propagate.

**Why this matters:** Understanding steering mechanisms allows practitioners to (1) assess steering robustness, (2) diagnose failure cases (Braun et al., 2025), and (3) design better interventions with improved concept expression or reduced degradation (Da Silva et al., 2025; Feng et al., 2026).

**Key prior work referenced:**
- **Representation Engineering** (Zou et al., 2025) -- the broader framework of manipulating model representations
- **Activation Addition / Contrastive Activation Addition** (Turner et al., 2023; Rimsky et al., 2024) -- the basic mechanism of adding vectors to residual stream
- **Difference-in-Means (DIM)** (Belrose, 2023; Rimsky et al., 2024) -- contrastive averaging for steering vectors
- **Preference Optimization steering** (Cao et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2025b) -- learned steering via contrastive objectives
- **Circuit discovery via activation patching** (Wang et al., 2023; Mueller et al., 2025; Hanna et al., 2024) -- identifying causal subgraphs in transformer models
- **Sinii et al. (2025)** -- the closest prior work, applying causal analysis to reasoning steering, but limited to last 2 layers and only one steering methodology

## Architecture

### Multi-token Activation Patching Framework

The paper extends classical activation patching (Meng et al., 2022; Vig et al., 2020) from single-token tasks to **multi-token steered generation**. The key insight: for steering, the "clean" and "corrupt" inputs are *identical prompts* but with different hidden states due to the injected steering vector.

Let steering vector $\mathbf{s} \in \mathbb{R}^{d}$ be tiled across an $N$-length sequence as $S = ([\mathbf{s}] \times N)^{\top} \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times d}$. Base and steered representations at layer $\ell$:

$$H\_{base}^{\ell} \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times d}, \quad H\_{steer}^{\ell} = H\_{base}^{\ell} + \alpha \cdot S$$

The steered representation $H\_{steer}$ is treated as "clean" and $H\_{base}$ as "corrupt". Using **Edge Attribution Patching with Integrated Gradients (EAP-IG)** (Hanna et al., 2024), the indirect effect of edge $(u, v)$ is:

$$(u - u^{*})^{\top} \frac{1}{T}\left(\sum\_{i=1}^{T} \frac{\partial m(H\_{base}^{\ell} + \frac{i}{T}\alpha \cdot S)}{\partial v}\right)$$

where $T = 10$ integrated gradients steps, and $m$ is the **logit difference** importance metric: $m(x') = \text{logit}(y \mid x') - \text{logit}(y^{*} \mid x')$.

### Computational Graph and Circuit Construction

The model is treated as a **directed acyclic computational graph**. Nodes $u$ consist of embedding matrix, MLP submodules, and MHA submodules. Edges $(u, v)$ span from output of upstream node $u$ to input of downstream node $v$.

- Only edges from layers $\geq$ the steering layer are considered (prior activations are identical)
- $\mid M \mid = 8{,}790$ total edges for Gemma 2 2B (steering at layer 15: 2,212 steered edges)
- $\mid M \mid = 124{,}441$ total edges for Llama 3.2 3B (steering at layer 12: 12,849 steered edges)
- Circuits $C$ are extracted via **greedy graph search** (Mueller et al., 2025; see Appendix D.2)
- **Faithfulness** metric: $(m(C) - m(\emptyset)) / (m(M) - m(\emptyset))$ where $\emptyset$ is the empty set (base model)

### Steering Value Vector (SVV) Decomposition

The paper decomposes the direct effect of steering vector $\mathbf{s}$ on attention head outputs. For head $h$ at layer $\ell \geq$ steering layer, with RMSNorm weights $\gamma$:

$$\text{svv}^{h}(\mathbf{s}) = (\mathbf{s} \odot \gamma) W\_{OV}^{h} \in \mathbb{R}^{d}$$

This is the **steering value vector** -- it arises through the **OV circuit** and is **input-invariant** (conditioned only on the steering vector, not on the input). The full attention decomposition:

$$\text{Attention}(H^{\ell} + \alpha \cdot S) = \sum\_{h} A^{h} D\_{c} \tilde{H}^{\ell} W\_{OV}^{h} + D\_{c^{h}} \text{svv}^{h}(S)$$

The first term is the original (unsteered) attention; the second term is the **steering's direct effect through the OV circuit**. The QK circuit ($A^{h}$) does not directly interact with the steering vector in this decomposition.

## Training / Experiments

### Models

| Model | Size | Hidden dim $d$ | Steering Layer | Steering Position |
|-------|------|-----------------|----------------|-------------------|
| Gemma 2 2B Instruct | 2B | 2,048 | Layer 15 | Position -1 (last token) |
| Llama 3.2 3B Instruct | 3B | 3,072 | Layer 12 | Position -4 |

### Steering Methods Compared

Three steering methodologies, all applied at the same layer per model:

1. **Difference-in-Means (DIM)** -- contrastive activation averaging (non-learned):
   $$\mathbf{s} = \frac{1}{\mid D\_{harm} \mid}\sum\_{p \in D\_{harm}} \mathbf{h}\_{i}^{\ell}(p) - \frac{1}{\mid D\_{safe} \mid}\sum\_{q \in D\_{safe}} \mathbf{h}\_{i}^{\ell}(q)$$

2. **Next Token Prediction (NTP)** -- learned via language modeling loss on desired concept responses:
   $$\min \sum\_{x,y \in D^{+}} \left\{-\sum\_{i=1}^{k} \log p(y\_{i} \mid x, h^{\ell} \leftarrow h^{\ell} + \alpha v)\right\}$$
   Training on first 64 tokens only (refusal expressed early).

3. **Preference Optimization (PO)** -- learned via contrastive responses (uni-directional RePS, Wu et al., 2025b):
   $$\min \sum\_{x,y^{w},y^{l} \in D} \left\{-\log \sigma(\Delta\_{x,y^{w},y^{l}})\right\}$$

### Datasets

- **$D\_{harm}$ train**: harmful prompts from AdvBench, MaliciousInstruct, TDC2023, HarmBench (128 train / 32 val)
- **$D\_{safe}$ train**: harmless prompts from Alpaca (128 train / 32 val)
- **NTP/PO training**: augmented to 256 harmful + 256 harmless prompts with contrastive response pairs
- **Test**: 100 harmful prompts from JailbreakBench + 100 harmless prompts from Alpaca
- **Sparsity evaluation**: Alpaca augmented to 200 samples + StrongReject benchmark
- **Activation patching datasets**: ~80-99 responses per condition, ~8,000-12,000 token positions each

### Evaluation

- **Attack Success Rate (ASR)**: proportion of completions that bypassed refusal
- Alpaca (positive steering to induce refusal): evaluated via **refusal substring matching**
- JailbreakBench & StrongReject (negative steering to jailbreak): evaluated via **LlamaGuard 2 8B** judge
- For circuit discovery: **logit difference faithfulness** metric

## Evaluation

### Circuit Faithfulness

- **~10% of edges** (900/8,790) from Gemma 2 2B recover 85% average faithfulness
- **~11% of edges** (13,500/124,441) from Llama 3.2 3B recover 85% average faithfulness
- Circuit complement has near 0 faithfulness at all sizes
- Random circuits at 2$\times$ minimum-faithful size: **<10% faithfulness**

### Circuit Interchangeability

- Circuit overlap defined as $\mid C\_{1} \cap C\_{2} \mid / \min(\mid C\_{1} \mid, \mid C\_{2} \mid)$
- **Nearly 100% overlap** between smaller and larger circuits across DIM, NTP, PO
- **Strong faithfulness recovery** for each vector-circuit cross-permutation
- Despite functional interchangeability, raw **cosine similarities between steering vectors are modest: 0.10--0.42**

### Attention Ablation Results (Table 1, DIM vector)

| Ablation | Avg ASR Change |
|----------|---------------|
| **Freeze QK** (all layers) | **8.75%** (G2: +3% Alpaca, -6% JBB; L3: +11% Alpaca, -15% JBB) |
| **Freeze OV** (all layers) | **71.75%** (G2: +57% Alpaca, -74% JBB; L3: +96% Alpaca, -60% JBB) |
| **Ablate SVV only** | **53.75%** (G2: +35% Alpaca, -60% JBB; L3: +86% Alpaca, -38% JBB) |
| **Ablate MLP direct effect** | **44.50%** (G2: +29% Alpaca, -23% JBB; L3: +50% Alpaca, -76% JBB) |

Key findings:
- **SVV ablation accounts for 74.9% of the OV circuit's performance drop**
- Steering vector's effects on OV are largely through the SVV
- QK circuit is **largely irrelevant** to refusal steering

### Edge Distribution

Top 100 (Gemma 2) / top 1000 (Llama 3.2) edges by importance:
- **Almost no top edges connect to attention queries or keys**
- Edges primarily connect to **attention values, MLPs, and LM head**
- Suggests steering propagates through value projections and MLPs, not query/key pathways

### SVV Interpretability (Logit Lens)

- SVVs contain top tokens related to both **refusal and harmfulness**
- **Gemma 2 2B L16H1 SVV**: consistently reveals words synonymous with "forbidden" across all methods
- **Gemma 2 2B L25H6**: reveals harmful tokens for DIM and PO but **not NTP** (divergence in some heads)
- **Gemma 2 2B L17H6**: high *negative* IE score with incoherent top tokens; **sign flip reveals harmful tokens**, suggesting this head *removes* harmful concepts during steering (superposition inefficiency)
- **NTP raw steering vector is not interpretable via logit lens**, but SVV decomposition uncovers meaningful tokens
- **Llama 3.2 3B DIM raw vector also not interpretable**, but SVV works

### Sparsity Results

Sparsification methods tested: **gradient-based** ($r\_{i} < \tau$ for $\tau \in \{0, 0.1, 0.3, 0.5, 1.0, 1.5, 2.0, 2.5\}$), **IE-based** (drop bottom $k$ by $\vec{IE}$ magnitude), **bottom-$k$** (drop bottom $k$ by $\mathbf{s}$ magnitude), **random dropout**:

| Method | Gemma 2 2B | Llama 3.2 3B |
|--------|------------|--------------|
| Gradient / IE sparsification | Retains ASR up to **~90% sparsity** | Retains ASR up to **>~95% sparsity** |
| Bottom-$k$ | Retains ASR up to **~80% sparsity** | -- |
| Random dropout | Retains ASR up to **~40% sparsity** | -- |
| DIM on StrongReject (Llama) | -- | **Nearly constant ASR at 9/3,072 non-zero dims** ($\tau = 2.5$) |

**IoU analysis**: Intersection over Union of sparsified DIM, NTP, PO vectors is **statistically significant** ($p < 0.05$) at every $\tau > 0$, with $p \lesssim 10^{-10}$ at 20%--95% sparsity. This confirms different methods converge to a **shared low-dimensional subspace**.

## Reproduction Guide

### Step 1: Environment & Models
- Load **Gemma 2 2B Instruct** and **Llama 3.2 3B Instruct** from HuggingFace
- Gemma 2 2B has 26 layers, hidden dim 2,048; Llama 3.2 3B has 28 layers, hidden dim 3,072
- Both are pre-layernorm architectures

### Step 2: Steering Vector Construction
1. **DIM vector**: Collect 128 harmful prompts (AdvBench + MaliciousInstruct + TDC2023 + HarmBench) and 128 harmless prompts (Alpaca). Extract activations at steering layer, compute difference-in-means at the last post-instruction token (Gemma: layer 15 pos -1; Llama: layer 12 pos -4)
2. **NTP vector**: Augment to 256 harmful + 256 harmless, generate contrastive response pairs, train with LM loss on first 64 tokens. Grid search over $\alpha$ and learning rate
3. **PO vector**: Use same contrastive pairs, train with uni-directional RePS loss. Grid search over $\alpha$, learning rate, and temperature $\phi$

### Step 3: Multi-token Activation Patching
1. For each test prompt, generate greedy decoded responses with and without steering
2. **Filter** for samples where steering successfully flips concept expression
3. Teacher-force on the full response in a single forward pass
4. **Mask** token positions where steered and base models agree ($m(x') = 0$)
5. Run EAP-IG (Eq. 4) at each of $N$ token positions, sum scores per edge
6. Average IE across the dataset
7. Construct circuit via greedy graph search (Appendix D.2)

### Step 4: SVV Decomposition
For each attention head $h$ at each layer:
$$\text{svv}^{h}(\mathbf{s}) = (\mathbf{s} \odot \gamma) W\_{OV}^{h}$$
Apply logit lens (multiply by unembedding matrix) to interpret top tokens.

### Step 5: Sparsification
Compute dimension-level $\vec{IE}$ at steering layer node (Eq. 6), then compute $r = \vec{IE} / \mathbf{s}$. Zero out dimensions where $r\_{i} < \tau$.

### Required Compute
- Running EAP-IG on Gemma 2 2B: manageable on a single GPU (small model)
- Llama 3.2 3B: 124,441 edges with 10 IG steps each -- moderately expensive
- Learning NTP/PO vectors: lightweight (optimizing a single $d$-dimensional vector, not fine-tuning the model)
- **No code released yet** (noted in paper footnote: "Code will be released upon publication")

### Known Gotchas
- Must mask positions where steered and base models agree (zero steering signal)
- Training NTP/PO on first 64 tokens only gives significantly better performance (not full 512)
- For PO, loss depends on $\phi$, so you cannot directly compare validation loss across different $\phi$ values -- use "match score" instead
- Cosine similarity is **not informative** on sparse high-dimensional vectors; use IoU instead
- Edge counts differ dramatically between models due to different numbers of attention heads and MLP intermediate dimensions

## Notes

### Connections to Binh's Research Interests

**Contrastive activation averaging + PCA denoising**: The DIM vector is precisely contrastive activation averaging. The paper shows this simple approach produces steering vectors whose circuits overlap nearly 100% with learned (NTP, PO) vectors -- suggesting contrastive averaging captures the core concept direction effectively. The SVV decomposition could be viewed as a form of structured projection (through $W\_{OV}^{h}$) that denoises the raw steering vector into interpretable concepts.

**SAE decomposition**: The SVV decomposition effectively projects the steering vector through each head's OV matrix, decomposing it into head-specific concept contributions. This is analogous to SAE feature decomposition but uses the model's own weight matrices rather than learned dictionaries. The finding that the SVV is "input-invariant" parallels SAE features being directionally interpretable.

**Activation steering by clamping features**: The sparsification results (90--99% sparsity while retaining performance) directly support feature-clamping approaches. The IoU analysis confirms a shared low-dimensional subspace across methods -- exactly the kind of structure you'd want to clamp. The gradient-based sparsification method ($r\_{i} < \tau$) provides a principled way to identify which dimensions to clamp.

**Emotion vectors / persona vectors**: Chen et al. (2025) show persona vectors work via similar contrastive averaging. This paper's finding that all steering methods converge to the same circuit pathway regardless of training method suggests **persona vectors, emotion vectors, and refusal vectors likely share the same OV-dominant mechanism**. The SVV decomposition should generalize to these other steering targets.

**Key takeaway for activation steering research**: Steering vectors work primarily through the OV circuit, not QK. This means steering is largely about **injecting specific content directions** into the value stream rather than rerouting attention patterns. The steering value vector ($\text{svv}^{h} = (\mathbf{s} \odot \gamma) W\_{OV}^{h}$) is the fundamental unit of steering -- it is input-invariant, semantically interpretable, and accounts for the majority of steering's effect on attention. Future steering methods could directly optimize SVVs for cleaner concept expression.
