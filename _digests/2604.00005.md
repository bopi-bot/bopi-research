## problem

emotion prompting and emotion steering are two existing approaches to injecting emotion into LLMs, but both are surface-level. emotion prompting adds emotion labels to the input text (e.g. "answer this in a happy tone"), which treats emotion as a style factor. emotion steering adds vectors to hidden states, but typically uses generic directions (e.g. "happiness vector") without grounding them in the model's internal representations. neither approach examines the mechanistic role of emotion in task processing -- how specific emotional states affect reasoning accuracy, safety behavior, or multi-step agent planning.

E-STEER closes this gap by mapping the VAD (Valence, Arousal, Dominance) psychological model onto SAE-decomposed latent representations, enabling precise, interpretable, and continuous control over emotional states in hidden states. the framework lets you dial emotion like a slider (valence from -9 to +9) and observe non-monotonic effects on task performance.

## architecture

three-stage pipeline:

**stage 1 -- emotional latent space construction**: a pre-trained SAE encodes hidden states at layer $k$ into a sparse latent space. the SAE encoder/decoder are $z = f\_{\text{enc}}(h\_k)$, $h'\_k = f\_{\text{dec}}(z)$. the model used is Qwen3-8B with SAE attached at $k = 17$ (validation on gpt-oss-20B with SAE at $k = 11$).

**stage 2 -- VAD-based feature extraction**: uses a positive-negative contrastive procedure. for each sample, the same task description is given with different emotional labels (e.g. "happy" vs "sad"). hidden states are encoded through the SAE, and the activation difference per neuron between positive-negative pairs is computed. the top 50 neurons with largest activation differences are selected as candidates; those with stable patterns across multiple pairs are retained as the final emotional latent representation. this is done separately for each VAD dimension (valence, arousal, dominance), yielding three distinct neuron subsets.

**stage 3 -- emotional steering**: the steering direction for emotion $e\_i$ is computed from the mean difference vector of its neuron subset, normalized by the original hidden state magnitude:

$$\tilde{d}\_i = \frac{d\_i}{\|d\_i\|} \cdot \|h\_k\| \cdot \frac{\delta\_i}{\delta\_{\max}}$$

where $d\_i = \hat{u}\_i$ is the unit vector in the latent space spanning the emotion's neuron subset, $\delta\_i$ is the VAD parameter value ($-9$ to $+9$), and $\delta\_{\max}$ normalizes it. the final steered hidden state is:

$$\tilde{h}\_k = h\_k + \alpha \cdot \sum\_{e\_i \in \{e\_v, e\_a, e\_d\}} \tilde{d}\_i$$

implemented via forward hooks -- the hidden state after layer $k$ is intercepted, modified, and re-injected. this is direction-based steering (additive), not replacement steering -- it preserves task-irrelevant information in the original representation.

## training

- base model: Qwen3-8B
- SAE: pre-trained (likely GemmaScope-style or custom, attached to residual stream at layer $k = 17$)
- contrastive pairs: same task description, different emotion labels, to identify VAD-responsive neurons
- top 50 candidate neurons per VAD dimension, filtered for stability across pairs
- steering strength $\alpha$ is a hyperparameter (not specified in main text)
- VAD range: $-9$ to $+9$ in integer steps
- no fine-tuning of the base model -- pure inference-time steering
- validation: gpt-oss-20B with SAE at layer $k = 11$

## evaluation

four task categories, each evaluated across all three VAD dimensions:

**LLM objective behavior** (reasoning accuracy):
- datasets: LogiQA 2.0 (logic), HumanEval (code), Math (quantitative/scientific)
- positive valence: 33.1% higher AVR (Answer Validity Rate) than negative valence
- arousal and dominance show U-shaped pattern (trough at arousal = $-3$, dominance = $+3$)
- TSR (Task Success Rate) peaks at valence = $+3$ to $+6$ (3.4% avg improvement over neutral)
- moderate arousal ($+3$): 4.7% TSR improvement
- optimal dominance shifts with task difficulty (easier tasks: higher dominance better)

**LLM subjective behavior** (text generation):
- dataset: TinyStories
- relevance: +33.6% at arousal = $-3$
- coherence: +33.6% at dominance = $+3$
- creativity: +6.5% at valence = $+3$
- conciseness: +23.3% negative vs positive
- overall: up to 14.5% improvement over neutral states

**LLM safety**:
- dataset: HarmBench
- safety AVR consistent with subjective tasks
- high dominance ($+6$) causes generic "I cannot answer" refusals
- specific emotions can improve safety by making the model more careful without excessive refusal

**agent behavior** (multi-step planning/execution):
- datasets: HotpotQA, Scientific, GAIA
- planning: plan validity rate peaks at valence = $-3$ (+33.2% improvement), arousal = $-3$
- positive dominance: +79.8% improvement in planning over negative states
- decision-making: replan frequency and trigger confidence follow inverted-U patterns
- emotional biases accumulate along multi-step reasoning chains

**emotion control fidelity** (Table 1):
- E-STEER vs prompt-level emotion control (Pearson correlation with target VAD values):
  - valence: 0.9816 vs 0.9437
  - arousal: 0.9792 vs 0.9021
  - dominance: 0.9206 vs 0.7756
- average improvement: +10.4% in Pearson correlation

**cross-model validation**: trends consistent on gpt-oss-20B, confirming findings are not model-specific.

**dataset-level robustness**: tested on ProntoQA, MBPP+, PHYBench, WritingPrompts, JailbreakBench -- emotion-behavior trends remain consistent.

**sampling robustness**: greedy vs stochastic sampling produce identical trends.

## reproduction guide

1. install the E-STEER framework (code not yet released as of the paper)
2. obtain a pre-trained SAE for your target model (Qwen3-8B, layer 17)
3. construct contrastive pairs: same task, different emotion labels (happy/sad for valence, calm/excited for arousal, submissive/dominant for dominance)
4. encode pairs through SAE, compute activation differences, select top 50 neurons
5. filter for stability across multiple pairs
6. implement forward hook at layer $k$ to intercept and modify hidden states
7. sweep $\delta\_i$ from $-9$ to $+9$ and measure task metrics

**gotchas**: VAD dimensions are not strictly orthogonal -- disentangling individual effects is inherently limited. extreme values ($\pm 9$) can cause generation instability or unparsable outputs on safety-aligned models. the optimal layer $k$ varies by model architecture.

## notes

this paper is the most direct mechanistic study of emotion in LLMs to date. the key insight is that emotions are not monotonically beneficial or harmful -- they follow inverted-U patterns where moderate levels are optimal. this aligns with the Yerkes-Dodson law from psychology (arousal-performance relationship).

the SAE-based approach is cleaner than Anthropic's emotion vectors (2026) because it identifies specific latent neurons for each VAD dimension rather than using contrastive averaging over activation differences. however, Anthropic's approach doesn't require an SAE, making it more accessible. the tradeoff is interpretability vs. setup complexity.

the finding that low valence + low arousal improves agent planning (by 33.2%) is counterintuitive and practically important: calm, slightly negative emotional states make agents more systematic and thorough. this could directly inform activation steering for robotic agents that need careful planning.

the scale-aligned steering direction $\tilde{d}\_i$ is a clever solution to the problem of inconsistent steering effects across tasks (hidden state magnitudes vary by task). normalizing by $\|h\_k\|$ makes the steering magnitude proportional to the representation, which is more robust than a fixed-magnitude additive steering.

connection to existing notes: this directly reinforces the "emotion representations locally scoped transformers" note and the "contrastive activation averaging extracts concept vectors" note. it also connects to the "can linear concept vectors steer model alignment" angle by providing concrete evidence that structured emotion vectors do steer model behavior in predictable, non-monotonic ways.
