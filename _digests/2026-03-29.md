    title: "LeWorldModel: Stable End-to-End Joint-Embedding Predictive Architecture from Pixels"
    authors: ["Lucas Maes", "Quentin Le Lidec", "Damien Scieur", "Yann LeCun", "Randall Balestriero"]
    categories: ["world-models", "JEPA", "robotics"]
    published: "2026-03-28"
    abs: "https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.19312"
    pdf: "https://arxiv.org/pdf/2603.19312"
    code: "https://github.com/lucas-maes/le-wm"
    problem: "Joint Embedding Predictive Architectures (JEPAs) are a compelling framework for learning world models in latent spaces, but existing methods are fragile. they rely on complex multi-term losses, exponential moving averages, pre-trained encoders, or auxiliary supervision to avoid representation collapse. no one has built a JEPA that trains stably end-to-end from raw pixels with a simple loss."
    methods: "LeWorldModel (LeWM) is the first JEPA that trains stably end-to-end from pixels using only two loss terms: a next-embedding prediction loss and a regularizer enforcing Gaussian-distributed latent embeddings. this reduces tunable loss hyperparameters from six to one compared to the only existing end-to-end alternative. ~15M parameters, trainable on a single GPU in a few hours."
    evaluation: "plans up to 48x faster than foundation-model-based world models while remaining competitive across diverse 2D and 3D control tasks. the latent space encodes meaningful physical structure verified through probing of physical quantities. surprise evaluation confirms the model reliably detects physically implausible events."
    reproduction: "code available at github.com/lucas-maes/le-wm. ~15M params, single GPU, few hours training."
    notes: "the key insight is that representation collapse can be avoided with just a Gaussian prior regularizer instead of the complex machinery other JEPAs use. this is a strong contribution because simplicity in the loss function directly translates to reproducibility and ease of use. the 48x speedup over foundation models while staying competitive is significant for robotics where iteration speed matters. connection to binh's world model learning repo: this paper answers Q1 (pixel representation) and Q4 (prediction) with a clean end-to-end approach. potential research angle: test LeWM on more complex manipulation tasks and compare against diffusion-based world models on real robot data."
