## Problem

Why does upgrading vision encoders not reliably improve VLA manipulation performance the way it improves VLM performance? The paper introduces the **Compression Gap** -- a principle showing that scaling behavior in any visuomotor pipeline is governed by the location of the **tightest information bottleneck**, not by uniformly adding capacity.

The core question: if you swap ResNet-18 for SigLIP (64-d → 1152-d features) in a VLA, does manipulation success go up? The answer depends entirely on the action representation:

- **Continuous action representations** (Diffusion Policy, flow matching): the vision encoder is the binding bottleneck. Upgrading it propagates end-to-end. Gains of **+21 to +26 percentage points** observed.
- **Discrete action tokenization** (OAT, FAST, binning, VQ-BeT): the codebook becomes the binding bottleneck. Encoder upgrades are blocked at quantization. Gains attenuated to **+3.6 to +10.4 percentage points**.

This is not a failure of any particular tokenizer -- it is a structural consequence of the data processing inequality applied to any pipeline with a fixed-capacity discrete bottleneck. The paper validates on LIBERO-10 with three lines of evidence: (1) a factorial experiment, (2) an encoder quality gradient across four encoders, and (3) a codebook capacity variation providing causal evidence.

Single author (Takuya Shiba, Shibattic Inc.). Project page: https://shibattic.github.io/compression-gap. Notably, Tong et al. (2026) independently observe the same structural asymmetry at pretraining scale (trillion-token multimodal pretraining): semantic encoders continue to scale while VAE-based representations saturate. This convergence suggests the Compression Gap is a general property of information-processing pipelines, not specific to robotics.

## Architecture

### The Compression Gap Principle (Information-Theoretic)

The framework considers the standard visuomotor information pathway $O \rightarrow Z \rightarrow A$, where $O$ is raw observation, $Z$ is the learned representation, and $A$ is the executed action. By the **data processing inequality**:

$$I(O; A) \leq \min\big(I(O; Z),\; I(Z; A)\big)$$

The mutual information between observation and action is bounded by the **tightest bottleneck**. Upgrading a non-binding bottleneck has no effect on end-to-end information flow.

**Continuous pathway (Diffusion Policy):**
$$O \xrightarrow{f\_{\mathrm{enc}}} Z \xrightarrow{\epsilon\_{\theta}} A$$

$I(Z; A)$ has no hard combinatorial bound -- limited only by network capacity and $Z$ quality. The binding bottleneck is $I(O; Z)$, i.e., the vision encoder. Upgrading the encoder directly widens the bottleneck.

**Discrete pathway (OAT):**
$$O \xrightarrow{f\_{\mathrm{enc}}} Z \xrightarrow{Q} T \xrightarrow{\mathcal{T}^{-1}} A$$

A quantization stage $Q$ inserts a hard bound:
$$I(O; A) \leq I(Z; T) \leq \log\_{2} \mid \mathcal{V} \mid ^{H\_l} = H\_l \log\_{2} \mid \mathcal{V} \mid $$

For OAT with $ \mid \mathcal{V} \mid  = 1000$ and $H\_l = 8$, this is approximately **80 bits per action chunk**. Once the codebook is saturated, upgrading the encoder cannot increase $I(O; A)$ -- the extra information is discarded at quantization.

### Action Representation Details

**Discrete: Ordered Action Tokenization (OAT).** Two-stage framework: (1) tokenizer $\mathcal{T}$ compresses continuous action chunk $a\_{1:H\_a}$ into $H\_l \ll H\_a$ discrete tokens via a transformer encoder with register tokens + FSQ (levels [8,5,5,5], $ \mid \mathcal{V} \mid  = 1000$); (2) autoregressive policy generates tokens sequentially $p(T\_{1:H\_l} \mid o\_{1:H\_o}) = \prod\_{i=1}^{H\_l} p(T\_i \mid T\_{<i}, o\_{1:H\_o})$.

**Continuous: Diffusion Policy (DP).** Models action distribution as denoising from $a^T \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I)$ with DDIM scheduler (100 training steps, 10 inference steps). Entire pipeline in continuous space -- no discretization stage.

**Other discrete schemes mentioned:** FAST (frequency-domain compression, BPE), per-dimension binning (RT-1, RT-2), VQ-BeT, QueST, $\pi\_0$ (continuous flow matching). The paper argues all discrete schemes with fixed-capacity codebooks are subject to the same bottleneck.

## Training

### Experimental Design

**Factorial experiment:** $2 \times 2 \times 2 = 8$ conditions varying:
- **Action representation:** OAT vs. Diffusion Policy
- **Vision encoder:** ResNet-18 (64-d, spatial softmax) vs. SigLIP (1152-d, pre-cached)
- **Model size:** M (4-layer, d=256, 4 heads) vs. L (6-layer, d=384, 6 heads), head dim = 64

**Encoder quality gradient:** M size on LIBERO-10, four encoders: ResNet-18 (64-d), SigLIP (1152-d), DINOv2 ViT-L/14 (1024-d), SigLIP 2 (1152-d).

**Codebook size variation:** M size on LIBERO-10, three FSQ configs: $ \mid \mathcal{V} \mid  = 1000$ ([8,5,5,5], ~80 bits/chunk), $ \mid \mathcal{V} \mid  = 1920$ ([8,8,6,5], ~87 bits/chunk), $ \mid \mathcal{V} \mid  = 4375$ ([7,5,5,5,5], ~97 bits/chunk).

### Benchmark and Implementation

- **LIBERO** (Franka Emika Panda), 7-dof action space (3D pos + 3D ori + 1D gripper)
- **LIBERO-10:** 10 tasks, 50 demonstrations per task
- Action chunk horizon $H\_a = 32$, execute first 16 steps before re-inference
- **Codebase:** Official OAT codebase (Liu et al., 2026) -- DP baseline from same codebase for fairness
- **Optimizer:** AdamW, constant LR: $5 \times 10^{-5}$ (policy), $1 \times 10^{-5}$ (obs encoder), no weight decay
- **Training:** 300 epochs, single NVIDIA A100 per run
- **Evaluation:** success rate every 50 epochs, 500 rollouts per eval (50 per task)
- **Metric:** peak success rate across training (matches OAT codebase convention)
- Vision features: ResNet-18 default (spatial softmax), SigLIP/DINOv2/SigLIP 2 pre-cached offline
- **Limitation acknowledged:** no multi-seed variance reporting; single benchmark

## Evaluation

### Table 1: Factorial Experiment (LIBERO-10, Peak Success Rate %)

| Action Repr. | Size | ResNet-18 | SigLIP | $\Delta\_{\mathrm{enc}}$ |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| DP | M | 36.4 | 57.6 | **+21.2** |
| DP | L | 44.0 | 70.0 | **+26.0** |
| OAT | M | 53.8 | 57.4 | +3.6 |
| OAT | L | 48.0 | 58.4 | +10.4 |

Key findings:
- DP encoder gains ($\Delta\_{\mathrm{enc}}$) are **5.9× to 7.2×** larger than OAT's
- OAT's attenuated gains persist across both model sizes -- ruling out capacity as confound
- DP benefits from M→L scaling with SigLIP (+12.4 pp) but not ResNet (+7.6 pp) -- consistent with encoder being binding constraint under continuous pathway
- OAT shows no consistent scaling gain across encoders -- autoregressive capacity cannot bypass codebook bottleneck

### Table 2: Encoder Quality Gradient (LIBERO-10, M Size)

| Encoder | Dim | DP | OAT | DP–OAT gap |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| ResNet-18 | 64 | 36.4 | 53.8 | OAT leads by **+17.4** |
| SigLIP | 1152 | 57.6 | 57.4 | tied |
| SigLIP 2 | 1152 | 62.8 | 44.2 | DP leads by **+18.6** |
| DINOv2 ViT-L/14 | 1024 | 63.8 | 51.0 | DP leads by **+12.8** |

Key findings:
- DP tracks encoder quality **monotonically**: 36.4 → 57.6 → 62.8 → 63.8
- OAT fluctuates without trend: 53.8 → 57.4 → 44.2 → 51.0
- **Crossover:** at weak encoder (ResNet-18), OAT beats DP by 17.4 pp; at strong encoder (DINOv2), DP beats OAT by 12.8 pp -- reversal of ~30 pp
- DP plateaus in low 60s even with best encoders, suggesting encoder becomes next binding constraint once discrete bottleneck is removed

### Table 3: Codebook Size (LIBERO-10, M Size, Causal Evidence)

| $|\mathcal{V}|$ | FSQ Levels | Bits/chunk | ResNet-18 | SigLIP | $\Delta\_{\mathrm{enc}}$ |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1000 | [8,5,5,5] | ~80 | 53.8 | 57.4 | +3.6 |
| 1920 | [8,8,6,5] | ~87 | 42.6 | 57.8 | **+15.2** |
| 4375 | [7,5,5,5,5] | ~97 | 54.6 | 58.6 | +4.0 |

Key findings:
- At $ \mid \mathcal{V} \mid  = 1000$: both encoders produce similar SR (53.8 vs 57.4) -- codebook masks encoder quality
- At $ \mid \mathcal{V} \mid  = 1920$: **striking asymmetry** -- SigLIP stable at 57.8%, ResNet drops to 42.6% (approaching DP's 36.4%). Codebook relaxed, true encoder quality exposed
- At $ \mid \mathcal{V} \mid  = 4375$: non-monotonic recovery (modelability--capacity tradeoff), but $ \mid \mathcal{V} \mid  = 1920$ result provides causal evidence
- The paper notes larger codebooks only partially recover sensitivity -- suggests hybrid approaches needed

## Reproduction Guide

1. **Clone the OAT codebase** (Liu et al., 2026, arxiv:2602.04215): `git clone https://github.com/xxx/oat` (use official repo linked from paper)
2. **Set up LIBERO-10:** install LIBERO benchmark, download 10-task dataset (50 demos/task), Franka Panda simulator environment
3. **Pre-cache vision features:** extract and cache features for SigLIP, DINOv2 ViT-L/14, SigLIP 2 offline (required for training efficiency)
4. **Configure policy backbone:**
   - Size M: 4-layer transformer decoder, d=256, 4 heads, head\_dim=64
   - Size L: 6-layer transformer decoder, d=384, 6 heads, head\_dim=64
5. **Configure OAT tokenizer:** FSQ with levels [8,5,5,5] for default $ \mid \mathcal{V} \mid  = 1000$; modify for codebook size experiments ([8,8,6,5] for 1920, [7,5,5,5,5] for 4375)
6. **Configure Diffusion Policy:** DDIM scheduler, 100 training steps, 10 inference steps
7. **Training:** AdamW optimizer, LR $5 \times 10^{-5}$ (policy) / $1 \times 10^{-5}$ (obs encoder), no weight decay, 300 epochs, single A100
8. **Evaluation:** every 50 epochs, 500 rollouts (50 per task), report peak success rate
9. **Key comparison:** run all 8 factorial conditions, then encoder gradient (4 encoders × 2 action repr.), then codebook size (3 sizes × 2 encoders)
10. **Single-GPU requirement:** each run uses 1× A100. Full experiment matrix: 8 (factorial) + 6 (gradient) + 6 (codebook) = ~20 runs

## Notes

### Key Takeaways

1. **The binding bottleneck governs scaling.** This is the most important insight. In any visuomotor pipeline, you must identify where the tightest information bottleneck is before investing in scaling. Upgrading a non-binding stage is wasted compute.

2. **Discrete tokenization creates a hard ceiling of ~80 bits/chunk.** OAT's FSQ codebook ($ \mid \mathcal{V} \mid  = 1000$, $H\_l = 8$) can transmit at most $\log\_2(1000^8) \approx 80$ bits per action chunk. Once saturated, no upstream improvement matters.

3. **The crossover effect is striking.** OAT beats DP by 17.4 pp with ResNet-18 but DP beats OAT by 12.8 pp with DINOv2. The relative advantage of discrete vs. continuous is **not intrinsic** -- it depends on upstream encoder quality. Discrete tokenization is a crutch for weak perception; with strong perception it becomes a liability.

4. **Connection to pretraining-scale findings.** Tong et al. (2026) observe the same asymmetry at trillion-token scale: VAE latents saturate while semantic encoders (RAE/SigLIP 2) continue to improve. The Compression Gap appears to be a general property, not robotics-specific.

5. **DP also plateaus at low 60s.** Even with the best encoders, DP caps around 63.8%. The paper suggests this means the encoder becomes the next binding constraint -- and existing encoders (designed for semantic understanding, not manipulation) may not capture contact geometry, affordances, spatial dynamics.

### Connection to unified-discrete-token-space-vla

This paper casts serious doubt on the unified-discrete-token-space paradigm for VLA scaling. The unified approach (tracking in notes 2603.25406, 2603.25661, and the DFM-VLA digest at 2603.26320) maps language, images, and actions into **one discrete token space** to enable single-model generation with prefix-based decoding and LLM integration.

The Compression Gap directly predicts this approach will hit a scaling wall:

- **Any fixed discrete codebook for actions creates a binding bottleneck.** Whether it's FSQ, BPE, binning, or VQ-VAE, if $I(Z; T) \leq H\_l \log\_2  \mid \mathcal{V} \mid $ is the tightest constraint, upgrading the vision encoder (or language model, or any upstream component) will yield diminishing returns.
- The unified approach makes this worse because it must share codebook capacity across **multiple modalities** (language + vision + action), potentially making the per-modality bottleneck even tighter.
- The paper explicitly notes: *"discrete actions remain valuable for unified language-action modeling with pretrained LLMs and prefix-based decoding"* but immediately qualifies this with the codebook capacity finding.

**However**, the paper also notes a path forward: *"the bottleneck is codebook capacity, not discreteness itself, since larger codebooks partially recover encoder sensitivity."* The unified approach could work if: (a) codebook capacity is made very large (but then you lose the compactness benefit of discrete tokens), or (b) hybrid architectures pair discrete reasoning with continuous action decoding.

The DFM-VLA angle (2603.26320) uses discrete flow matching for iterative refinement -- but if actions are still in a fixed discrete space, the Compression Gap still applies. Flow matching helps with generation quality within the token space, but cannot exceed the information capacity of that space.

**Bottom line:** this paper provides theoretical grounding for the skepticism in the `can-vla-models-eliminate-separate-world-models` angle. Any VLA architecture that funnels actions through a fixed discrete bottleneck will hit a scaling ceiling. The path to scalable Physical AI requires continuous (or very high-capacity) action representations that allow each pipeline component to improve independently.

### Limitations

- Single benchmark (LIBERO-10), no real-robot validation
- No multi-seed variance -- results are peak SR, not mean±std
- Codebook size experiment shows non-monotonicity (1920 vs 4375), suggesting interactions beyond pure capacity
- OAT values not perfectly monotonic across encoder gradient (44.2% with SigLIP 2 is unexpectedly low)
- Does not test other discrete tokenizers (FAST, binning, VQ-BeT) directly -- only OAT
- Does not test flow matching ($\pi\_0$) or ACT as additional continuous baselines
